### Post 16: "Cap Table Mistakes"

**Hook:**
📋 I have seen cap tables that would make investors run. Here are the 5 most common mistakes Australian founders make.

**Body:**
Mistake 1: Verbal equity promises
"I told my co-founder they would get 20%." Unless it is in writing with vesting terms, it does not exist. Worse, it creates legal liability. Document every equity allocation from day one.

Mistake 2: No vesting schedule
Equal split on day one with no vesting means a co-founder can leave after a month and keep 50% of the company. Standard practice: 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff.

Mistake 3: Ignoring share classes
Ordinary shares are not the same as preference shares. When investors come in, you need proper share class structure. Set it up correctly from incorporation.

Mistake 4: No ESOP pool
Planning to hire? You will need an Employee Share Option Plan. Not having one means either diluting founders later or offering unattractive packages to talent.

Mistake 5: Spreadsheet cap tables
Google Sheets breaks at scale, has no audit trail, and does not model dilution scenarios. Use a proper tool.

BlockID.au offers a free cap table tool, equity split calculator, and dilution modeller -- all designed for Australian corporate structures.

### Post 19: "Equity Split Conversations"

**Hook:**
🤝 42% of co-founder relationships end in dispute. Most of those disputes start with the equity split conversation done wrong.

**Body:**
Here is how most equity splits happen:
"Let us just do 50/50."
"Sure."

And here is how most equity disputes happen:
"I have been doing all the work for 6 months and we still own the same amount."

The problem is not generosity. It is that a static, equal split does not reflect the dynamic reality of building a startup.

A better framework for equity conversations:

1. Agree on contribution categories
Who brings the idea? Who builds the product? Who sells? Who funds? Quantify each person's contribution.

2. Use a calculator, not feelings
BlockID's equity split tool weighs contribution, risk, time commitment, capital invested, and opportunity cost. It gives a recommended split based on data, not negotiation power.

3. Always include vesting
Even a perfectly fair split on day one needs vesting. Standard: 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff. This protects everyone.

4. Document it
Not in a text message. In a proper shareholder agreement. With board resolutions if you are a Pty Ltd.

5. Revisit quarterly
As roles evolve, so should the equity conversation. Use your cap table tool to model scenarios before having the discussion.

Fair equity is the foundation of a healthy startup. Get it right early.
