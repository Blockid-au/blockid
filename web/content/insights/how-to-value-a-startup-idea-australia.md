## Why valuation matters from day one

Most founders think valuation only matters when raising capital. That is wrong. Valuation is the foundation of every equity decision you make — co-founder splits, employee option pools, advisor grants, and investor negotiations.

In Australia, where the startup ecosystem is growing rapidly (over 2,600 active startups in 2025 according to [Startup Genome](https://startupgenome.com)), getting your valuation baseline right from the idea stage sets you up for every conversation that follows.

## The Australian startup valuation landscape

Australian startups face unique challenges compared to Silicon Valley:

- **Smaller market base** — 26 million people vs 330 million in the US
- **AUD vs USD** — currency fluctuation affects international comparisons
- **ESIC (Early Stage Innovation Company)** tax incentives change investor behaviour
- **Limited comparable exits** — fewer public reference points than US/UK markets
- **Strong government grants** — R&D Tax Incentive, Export Market Development Grants

These factors mean you cannot simply copy US valuation frameworks. You need an approach calibrated to the Australian market.

## Five methods to value an idea-stage startup

### 1. The Berkus Method (adapted for AUD)

Dave Berkus proposed assigning up to $500K USD per risk factor. For Australian startups, we adjust to AUD and local market conditions:

| Risk Factor | What It Measures | Max Value (AUD) |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Sound idea | Problem-solution fit | $250,000 |
| Working prototype | Technical risk reduction | $250,000 |
| Quality team | Execution capability | $500,000 |
| Strategic relationships | Market access | $250,000 |
| Product rollout / sales | Revenue traction | $500,000 |

**Total maximum pre-revenue valuation: AUD $1.75M**

This method works well when you have no revenue but can demonstrate progress across these dimensions.

> **Berkus Method Breakdown: Scoring Your Startup**
>
> | Risk Factor | Question to Answer | Score 0% | Score 50% | Score 100% (Max AUD) |
> | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
> | **Sound Idea** | Is there clear problem-solution fit? | No validated problem | Some customer interviews | Strong evidence of pain point ($250K) |
> | **Working Prototype** | Does a functional product exist? | Idea only | Basic MVP | Fully functional prototype ($250K) |
> | **Quality Team** | Can the team execute? | Solo non-technical founder | Partial team, some gaps | Complete team with domain expertise ($500K) |
> | **Strategic Relationships** | Do you have market access? | No partnerships | Early discussions | Signed LOIs or partnerships ($250K) |
> | **Product Rollout** | Is there revenue traction? | Pre-revenue | Beta users, no revenue | Paying customers, growing MRR ($500K) |
>
> **How to use:** Score each factor as a percentage of its maximum value, then sum the results. A startup scoring 60% across all factors would be valued at approximately **$1.05M AUD**.

### 2. Scorecard Method

Compare your startup to the "average" funded startup in your sector and adjust:

- Team strength: +/- 30%
- Market size: +/- 25%
- Product/technology: +/- 15%
- Competitive environment: +/- 10%
- Marketing/sales: +/- 10%
- Need for additional investment: +/- 5%
- Other factors: +/- 5%

**Baseline for Australian pre-seed:** AUD $1-2M (based on [AVCAL](https://www.avcal.com.au/) data for early-stage rounds in 2024-2025).

### 3. Venture Capital Method (reverse-engineered)

Work backwards from your expected exit:

1. **Estimate exit value** in 5-7 years (comparable exits in your sector)
2. **Apply target return multiple** (VCs want 10-30x for pre-seed)
3. **Calculate post-money valuation** = Exit Value / Target Multiple
4. **Subtract investment** to get pre-money valuation

Example: If a similar SaaS in Australia sold for AUD $50M, and a VC wants 20x return on a $500K investment:
- Post-money = $50M / 20 = $2.5M
- Pre-money = $2.5M - $500K = **$2M valuation**

### 4. Cost-to-Duplicate Method

Calculate what it would cost someone to build what you have from scratch:

- Development time and cost
- Market research and validation
- Intellectual property (patents, trademarks)
- Brand and domain assets
- Early customer relationships

This method provides a **floor valuation** — the absolute minimum your company is worth.

## What investors actually look at in Australia

Based on data from Australian accelerators and angel networks:

**Pre-seed (AUD $50K - $500K rounds):**
- Team composition and commitment (full-time vs part-time)
- Problem validation (customer interviews, waitlist, LOIs)
- Market size in Australia and expansion potential (APAC)
- ESIC eligibility (significant tax benefits for investors)
- Cap table cleanliness (no messy convertible notes)

**Seed (AUD $500K - $3M rounds):**
- All of the above, plus:
- Revenue or strong engagement metrics
- Clear unit economics path
- Data room readiness (term sheet, shareholders agreement, financial model)
- Governance structure (board, advisory, ESOP)

## Common valuation mistakes Australian founders make

1. **Valuing based on US comparables without adjustment** — A B2B SaaS in Sydney is not valued the same as one in San Francisco
2. **Ignoring cap table structure** — Messy cap tables with informal arrangements destroy value
3. **No evidence trail** — Investors want verifiable data, not just pitch deck claims
4. **Overvaluing at pre-seed** — An $8M valuation with no revenue makes your seed round nearly impossible
5. **Not tracking value creation** — If you cannot show how your value increased over 6 months, investors will not believe your projections
