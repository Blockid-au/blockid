## How to Value Your Australian Startup in 2026: 5 Methods VCs Actually Use

Ask five Australian investors how they value a pre-revenue startup and you will get five different answers. That is not because valuation is arbitrary — it is because different methods are appropriate at different stages, for different business models, and for different investor mandates. The Antler Investment Committee uses a different lens than a Blackbird seed partner, who uses a different lens than a super-angel writing their first cheque.

What every Australian founder needs to understand is that **valuation is not discovered — it is negotiated**. But you can only negotiate effectively if you understand the frameworks your investor is using. This guide breaks down the 5 valuation methods most commonly used by Australian VCs in 2026, with AU-specific benchmarks, deal size context, and how each method connects to your BlockID Startup Valuation Index (SVI) score.

## Method 1: The Scorecard Method

**Best for:** Pre-seed, idea-stage and pre-revenue startups
**Used by:** Angel investors, accelerators (Antler, Startmate), micro-VCs

The Scorecard Method, popularised by angel investor Bill Payne, starts with a regional baseline valuation and adjusts it up or down based on 5–7 weighted factors. In Australia in 2026:

**2026 AU Baseline Valuations:**
- Pre-seed (no product, no revenue): A$1.5M–$2.5M
- Pre-revenue with MVP: A$2.5M–$4M
- Seed with early traction: A$4M–$7M

**Scorecard weights (Australian adaptation):**

| Factor | Weight | Your Score | Adjusted Value |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :--- |
| Strength of founding team | 30% | 0.5–1.5x | Biggest single lever |
| Size of market opportunity | 25% | 0.5–1.5x | TAM must exceed A$500M |
| Product / technology | 15% | 0.5–1.5x | IP, defensibility, build quality |
| Competitive environment | 10% | 0.5–1.5x | Market crowding reduces score |
| Marketing / sales channels | 10% | 0.5–1.5x | Existing distribution is gold |
| Need for additional investment | 5% | 0.5–1.5x | Capital efficiency signals |
| Other factors | 5% | 0.5–1.5x | ESIC eligibility, ESOP, governance |

**Example:** A Sydney B2B SaaS startup with two ex-Atlassian founders, a clear wedge in a A$2B TAM, and 3 signed LOIs scores 1.3x on team, 1.2x on market — producing a pre-money valuation of roughly A$4.5M against a A$3M baseline.

## Method 2: The Berkus Method

**Best for:** Pre-revenue startups with a working prototype
**Used by:** Angel investors, family offices, early-stage syndicates

The Berkus Method assigns a fixed dollar value to each of five risk-reduction milestones. Originally designed in USD, the method scales approximately to AUD in the current market:

| Milestone Achieved | Value Added (AUD) |
| :--- | :---: |
| Sound idea (basic value) | Up to A$500K |
| Prototype / working product | Up to A$1M |
| Quality management team | Up to A$1M |
| Strategic relationships (customers, partners) | Up to A$1M |
| Product rollout / revenue | Up to A$1M |

**Maximum Berkus valuation pre-revenue:** A$4.5M. This is roughly consistent with the upper end of pre-seed valuations in the Australian market, which makes Berkus a useful sanity check.

**Limitation:** The Berkus Method does not account for market size, competitive dynamics, or capital efficiency. It is best used as a floor, not a ceiling.

## Method 3: The VC Method

**Best for:** Seed and Series A rounds with identifiable exit comparables
**Used by:** Blackbird, AirTree, Folklore, Square Peg, VC funds generally

The VC Method works backwards from an expected exit. The logic: an investor needs to know what return multiple they can expect given their entry valuation, and they estimate this by projecting your exit value and discounting it by their required return.

**The formula:**

```
Post-Money Valuation = Exit Value / (1 + Required Return)^Years to Exit
Pre-Money Valuation = Post-Money Valuation − Investment Amount
```

**AU-specific example (Seed round, B2B SaaS):**

- Projected exit in 5 years: A$60M (10x revenue multiple on A$6M ARR)
- Target return: 20x for a seed fund
- Implied post-money: A$60M ÷ 20 = A$3M
- Investment amount: A$1.5M
- Pre-money valuation: A$3M − A$1.5M = **A$1.5M**

This is why VC math often produces lower valuations than founders expect. The VC is not being conservative — they are pricing in the probability of failure. At seed stage, a top Australian VC expects roughly 65% of portfolio companies to return less than 1x. The winners must return 20-50x to make the fund work.

**Negotiating the VC Method:** The three levers you control are your projected exit revenue multiple, your exit timeline, and demonstrating a credible path to the exit scenario. Founders who can cite current Australian M&A multiples (typically 4–8x ARR for B2B SaaS) and named acquisition comparables negotiate better outcomes.

## Method 4: Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

**Best for:** Later-stage startups with 12+ months of revenue history
**Used by:** Series A+ investors, growth equity funds, strategic acquirers

DCF analysis projects future free cash flows and discounts them back to present value using a weighted average cost of capital (WACC). For startups, this is adapted because WACC is replaced by a **risk-adjusted discount rate** — typically 40–75% for early-stage companies, reflecting the high probability of failure.

**Why DCF is rarely the primary method before Series A:**
- Pre-revenue projections are almost always wrong
- A small change in the discount rate dramatically changes the output
- Australian VCs use DCF as a cross-check, not a primary signal

**When DCF becomes relevant:** If your startup has A$500K+ ARR, 3+ years of financial data, and is entering a Series A process with sophisticated institutional investors, expect to be asked to defend a 5-year financial model with DCF assumptions. ## Method 5: Market Comparables (Revenue Multiples)

**Best for:** Any stage with revenue; most useful at Seed and beyond
**Used by:** All VC and growth equity funds; essential for term sheet anchoring

The market comparables method values your startup as a multiple of revenue (ARR or annualised run rate). The multiple depends on growth rate, net revenue retention, gross margin, and market sentiment.

**2026 Australian SaaS benchmarks:**

| Stage | ARR | Growth Rate | Typical Multiple |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Pre-Seed / MVP | <$100K | 30%+ MoM | Not applicable |
| Seed | $100K–$500K | 20%+ MoM | 15–25x ARR |
| Series A | $500K–$3M | 3–5x YoY | 8–15x ARR |
| Series B | $3M–$15M | 2–3x YoY | 6–10x ARR |

**How to use comparables in a negotiation:** Research recent Australian funding announcements (Startup Daily, AFR's Street Talk), identify companies at your stage and sector, and cite their implied valuations. "Canva raised at X multiple" is not relevant. "Three B2B SaaS companies at our ARR raised at 12–18x in the last 6 months in Australia" is.
