## Due Diligence Checklist for Australian Startups: What Investors Will Ask

The moment an investor says "we're interested," the real work begins. Due diligence is the systematic process by which investors verify everything you have told them — and discover the things you haven't. For Australian founders, the due diligence process is shaped by local regulatory requirements from ASIC, the ATO, and the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), making preparation more nuanced than following a generic global checklist.

Founders who enter due diligence unprepared waste weeks chasing documents, create doubt in investors' minds, and sometimes lose deals entirely. Founders who prepare proactively close faster, negotiate from strength, and build long-term trust with their investors.

This checklist covers every category an Australian investor will probe, from legal structure to financial records to IP ownership.

> **Key Insight:** According to AVCAL data, deals that close in under 60 days from term sheet to investment almost always involve founders who had their data room pre-populated before investor conversations began. Preparation is not just efficiency — it signals operational maturity.

## Category 1: Legal Structure and Corporate Records

This is the first category investors check, and it is the most commonly disorganised. Australian startups must maintain records under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), and any gaps will raise immediate red flags.

**What investors will request:**
- ASIC company extract (current and historical)
- Constitution or replaceable rules confirmation
- Register of members (share register)
- Register of directors and company secretaries
- All shareholder agreements, including any prior versions
- Minutes of all board and shareholder meetings
- Any ASIC correspondence or notices received

**Common issues found:** Missing founder vesting agreements, unsigned shareholder resolutions, and discrepancies between the ASIC register and the internal share register.

## Category 2: Cap Table and Equity

Your cap table must be clean, accurate, and defensible. Investors will cross-reference it against ASIC filings, shareholder agreements, and any convertible instruments outstanding.

**What investors will request:**
- Full cap table showing all shareholders, share classes, and percentages
- Details of any convertible notes or SAFE notes outstanding (including terms, caps, discounts)
- All option and warrant grants, vesting schedules, and exercise prices
- Any rights of first refusal, drag-along, or tag-along provisions
- Advisor equity agreements and their current vesting status

**Red flags:** Founder shares not on a vesting schedule, undocumented verbal equity promises, advisor equity grants with no cliff, and SAFE notes with aggressive MFN clauses.

## Category 3: Financial Records

Australian investors will conduct financial due diligence aligned with ATO reporting obligations and standard accounting practices. Disorganised financials are the single biggest cause of deal delays.

**What investors will request:**
- Two to three years of financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow)
- Most recent management accounts (current to within 30 days)
- ATO tax lodgement history and BAS statements
- R&D Tax Incentive registration and prior claims (if applicable)
- Bank statements for the last 12 months
- Details of all outstanding liabilities, debts, and deferred revenue
- Revenue recognition policy and ARR/MRR breakdown for SaaS businesses

**ATO-specific flags:** Overdue BAS lodgements, unregistered GST (if turnover exceeds $75K threshold), director penalty notices, and unpaid super guarantee obligations.

## Category 4: Intellectual Property

For technology and product companies, IP ownership is often the most critical due diligence item. Investors need to verify that the company — not its founders, contractors, or employees — owns all the IP that underpins the product.

**What investors will request:**
- IP assignment agreements signed by all founders (especially those who built the product before company incorporation)
- Contractor agreements with IP assignment clauses
- Employee IP assignment clauses in all employment contracts
- Trademark registrations (IP Australia filings)
- Patent applications or grants, if any
- Open-source licence audit (any GPL/LGPL code in commercial product?)
- Domain name and social handle ownership verification

**Critical issue:** If a founder built the core product before the company was incorporated, and never formally assigned the IP to the company, investors cannot fund the business. This is the single most common IP red flag in Australian pre-seed deals.

## Category 5: Commercial Contracts and Revenue

**What investors will request:**
- All material customer contracts (especially any with unusual termination or exclusivity clauses)
- Supplier and vendor agreements
- Details of any revenue concentration (one customer representing more than 20% of ARR is a flag)
- Any outstanding disputes, claims, or letters of demand received
- Partnership agreements and referral arrangements

## Category 6: People and Employment

**What investors will request:**
- All employment contracts (particularly for key personnel)
- Confirmation of right to work for all employees in Australia (visa status)
- Details of any Fair Work Act disputes, unfair dismissal claims, or WorkCover incidents
- Superannuation guarantee compliance history
- Offer letters and any verbal equity promises not yet documented

## Category 7: Technology and Security

**What investors will request:**
- Code repository access or technical architecture overview
- Evidence of data security practices (especially if handling personal information under the Privacy Act 1988)
- Hosting and infrastructure agreements (AWS, Azure, GCP contracts)
- Any prior security incidents or breaches
- Third-party software licences and compliance

## Category 8: Regulatory and Compliance

**What investors will request:**
- ASIC lodgement history (annual reviews, changes notified on time)
- Any industry-specific licences (AFSL for fintech, TGA for healthtech, etc.)
- Privacy policy and data handling practices compliant with the Australian Privacy Principles
- ESIC eligibility assessment if angels are seeking the tax offset

## Pre-Seed vs Seed Due Diligence: What Changes

At pre-seed, investors are primarily checking that the legal foundation is clean and the team is credible. At seed and Series A, the scrutiny intensifies across all eight categories, with financial audits, independent IP opinions, and sometimes third-party technical reviews.

The smartest thing you can do is treat your company as if you are always 60 days from a due diligence process. Maintain clean records, keep your ASIC filings current, and never let your cap table drift from reality.

## Conclusion: Preparation Is Competitive Advantage

Due diligence is not a bureaucratic hurdle — it is an opportunity to demonstrate that your company is run with the rigour investors expect. Founders who arrive with a complete data room, clean ASIC records, and documented IP ownership close deals faster and on better terms.

The eight categories above cover the full spectrum of what Australian investors will examine. Work through them systematically, engage an Australian startup lawyer to identify gaps, and treat preparation as an ongoing discipline rather than a pre-raise scramble.
