## ESOP Setup Guide for Australian Startups in 2026: ESS Part 7A Compliance

Setting up an Employee Share Option Plan (ESOP) is one of the most impactful — and most frequently botched — structural decisions an Australian startup founder makes. Done correctly under the **ESS Part 7A** framework, an ESOP attracts and retains talent, aligns team incentives with long-term equity value, and signals governance maturity to investors. Done incorrectly, it creates unexpected tax liabilities for employees, messy cap table disputes, and red flags during due diligence.

This guide covers the complete ESOP setup process for Australian startups in 2026: the legislative framework, pool sizing standards, vesting mechanics, legal deed requirements, ASIC obligations, realistic cost estimates, and the most common mistakes founders make before their first raise.

> **Key Insight:** The ATO's **Division 83A** concessions — the tax deferral rules that make ESOPs attractive for startup employees — are only available if your scheme meets specific conditions under **ESS Part 7A** of the *Income Tax Assessment Act 1997*. A non-compliant scheme exposes your employees to upfront tax on unvested options, destroying the retention value you were trying to create.

## What Is ESS Part 7A and Why Does It Matter?

The **Employee Share Scheme (ESS)** legislation in Australia sits within Division 83A of the *Income Tax Assessment Act 1997*, commonly referred to as **ESS Part 7A**. The framework was substantially reformed in 2015 and again updated in 2022 to make startup equity more accessible and tax-effective.

Under ESS Part 7A, employees who receive options or shares under a qualifying scheme can **defer the taxing point** until one of three events occurs:

1. The options vest and are exercised
2. The employee leaves the company
3. A 15-year maximum deferral period expires

For startup employees — who might wait 4-7 years before any liquidity event — this deferral is critical. Without it, an employee could receive a $50,000 notional gain on unvested options and owe tax immediately, with no cash to pay it.

**To qualify for ESS Part 7A startup concessions, your company must:**
- Be an Australian resident company (or a foreign company with a permanent establishment in Australia)
- Have annual aggregated turnover of less than $50 million
- Have been incorporated for fewer than 10 years at the time of grant
- Not have shares listed on a recognised exchange
- Issue options or shares to employees (not contractors — a common mistake)

## The 12% Pool Standard: How Much Equity to Reserve

The most widely used ESOP pool size among Australian seed-stage startups is **12% of the fully diluted share count**, reserved pre-investment. Here is how this standard has emerged:

| Stage | Typical ESOP Pool | Rationale |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| Pre-Seed | 8–10% | Covers initial hires, advisors, early team |
| Seed | 10–15% (12% median) | Pre-diluted before investor entry; covers 12–24 months of hiring |
| Series A | 15–20% (often refreshed) | International expansion, senior leadership, engineering scale |

Blackbird Ventures and AirTree Ventures both publish standard term sheet language expecting a **10–15% option pool** pre-money, meaning the pool dilutes founders, not investors. Negotiating the pool size is one of the most financially significant moments in any term sheet negotiation — a 5% difference on a $5M pre-money valuation represents $250,000 in effective dilution.

## Vesting Mechanics: 4 Years, 1-Year Cliff

The Australian startup community has largely converged on a **4-year vesting schedule with a 1-year cliff** as the standard for employee options. Here is how it works:

- **Cliff:** No options vest until the employee has completed 12 months of continuous employment. If they leave before 12 months, they receive nothing.
- **Monthly vesting post-cliff:** After the cliff, options vest monthly (1/48th of the total grant per month) over the remaining 36 months.
- **Total vesting:** After 48 months (4 years), 100% of the grant is vested.

**Example:** An employee receives 48,000 options. After 12 months, 12,000 options vest (the cliff). Then 1,000 options vest each month for the next 36 months.

**Acceleration clauses** are common but must be explicitly documented in the deed. The two types are:
- **Single-trigger acceleration:** Options fully vest on a change of control (acquisition). Investors dislike this as it can complicate M&A.
- **Double-trigger acceleration:** Options accelerate only if there is both a change of control AND the employee is terminated without cause. This is the investor-preferred standard in Australia.

## Legal Deed Requirements: What You Must Document

A compliant ESOP is not just a spreadsheet of who gets how many options. It requires formal legal documentation. In 2026, Australian startups need the following:

### 1. ESS Deed (Option Plan Rules)
The master document governing the entire scheme. It must specify:
- Eligibility criteria (employees vs. contractors distinction)
- Total pool size and maximum options issuable
- Vesting schedule and conditions
- Exercise price calculation methodology
- Lapse conditions (what happens to unvested options when someone leaves)
- Board discretion on acceleration

### 2. Individual Option Agreements
Each employee grant requires a separate option agreement signed by both the company and the employee. This agreement sets out the individual's grant size, exercise price, vesting start date, and any role-specific conditions.

### 3. ATO Employer Notification
Under ESS Part 7A, companies must notify the ATO annually of all ESS interests granted. The **ESS Annual Report** is due by 14 August each year and covers all grants made in the prior financial year. Missing this deadline incurs penalties.

### 4. ASIC Class Order Relief
Options are technically financial products under the *Corporations Act 2001*. Issuing them to employees without an Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL) is permissible only if you comply with **ASIC Class Order \[CO 14/1000]** or the more recent instrument. Your deed must be structured to fit within this relief.

## ASIC Obligations: What Founders Overlook

Beyond the ATO obligations, ASIC has specific requirements that many founders miss:

- **Change of share structure:** Every time you issue options or convert them to shares, you must lodge an **ASIC Form 484** (Change to Company Details) within 28 days.
- **Register of option holders:** You must maintain a separate register of option holders distinct from your shareholder register.
- **Solvency resolution:** Directors must pass a solvency resolution within two months of the end of each financial year. If your ESOP creates undisclosed liabilities, this becomes relevant.

## The 5 Most Common ESOP Mistakes Australian Founders Make

**1. Granting options to contractors instead of employees.** ESS Part 7A concessions only apply to employees. Issuing options to a freelance developer or contract CFO with no employment agreement creates a tax liability for them and a governance problem for you. Fix: establish a formal employment contract before any grant, or use a separate advisory equity framework for non-employees.

**2. Setting the exercise price too low.** The exercise price must be at least equal to the market value of the underlying shares at the time of grant (for qualifying schemes). Setting an artificially low exercise price — especially for options granted just before a funding round — can trigger immediate taxation on the discount.

**3. Not having board minutes for each grant.** Every individual option grant requires a board resolution. Verbal agreements to "give someone equity" have no legal standing. If you cannot produce signed board minutes for every grant, your cap table is legally incomplete.

**4. Omitting good leaver / bad leaver provisions.** What happens to an employee's vested and unvested options when they resign? Are they a good leaver (illness, redundancy) or a bad leaver (resignation without notice, misconduct)? Without explicit provisions, this becomes a negotiated dispute — often at the worst possible time (pre-acquisition or Series A due diligence).

**5. Failing to update the ESOP pool before a funding round.** Investors expect the option pool to be established and sized pre-investment. Trying to create an ESOP during a funding round is a due diligence red flag and will delay your close by weeks.
