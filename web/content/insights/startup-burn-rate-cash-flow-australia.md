## Managing Startup Burn Rate in Australia: Essential Pre-Seed Cash Flow Tips
As a pre-seed startup founder in Australia, managing your burn rate is crucial to extending your runway and increasing your chances of success. In this article, we'll provide a practical framework for calculating and managing your burn rate, as well as offer tips on how to optimize your cash flow.

### Understanding Gross vs Net Burn Rate
When it comes to managing your startup's burn rate, it's essential to understand the difference between gross burn and net burn.

* **Gross burn** refers to the total amount of money your startup spends each month.
* **Net burn** refers to the amount of money your startup spends each month, minus any revenue it generates.

For example, if your startup spends $10,000 per month and generates $2,000 in revenue, your gross burn rate would be $10,000, and your net burn rate would be $8,000.

## Calculating Your Burn Rate
To calculate your burn rate, you'll need to track your startup's expenses and revenue over a specific period. Here's a simple formula to get you started:

1. Gather your startup's financial data for the past 3-6 months.
2. Calculate your total expenses (gross burn) for the period.
3. Calculate your total revenue for the period.
4. Subtract your revenue from your expenses to get your net burn rate.

### Default Alive Threshold
The concept of "default alive" was popularized by Paul Graham, co-founder of Y Combinator. It refers to the idea that a startup should have enough cash to survive for at least 12-18 months, even if it doesn't generate any revenue.

In Australia, the default alive threshold is particularly relevant. According to a survey by AVCAL, **65% of Australian startups fail due to cash flow issues**. To avoid this fate, it's essential to manage your burn rate and ensure you have enough cash to survive for at least 18 months.

## 18-Month Runway Rule
The 18-month runway rule is a simple yet effective way to ensure your startup has enough cash to survive. Here's how it works:

1. Calculate your net burn rate.
2. Multiply your net burn rate by 18.
3. Ensure you have enough cash to cover this amount.

For example, if your net burn rate is $10,000 per month, you should have at least **$180,000** in cash to survive for 18 months.

### When to Cut Costs vs Raise
When managing your burn rate, it's essential to know when to cut costs vs raise funds. Here are some general guidelines:

* **Cut costs** when:
	+ Your burn rate is too high.
	+ You're not generating enough revenue.
	+ You need to extend your runway.
* **Raise funds** when:
	+ You have a clear path to profitability.
	+ You've achieved significant traction.
	+ You need to accelerate growth.
