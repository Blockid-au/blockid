## The High Cost of Ignoring the Fine Print: A Founder's Guide to Term Sheet Red Flags

Raising capital is a milestone celebration, but it is also the most critical negotiation of your company's early life. For Australian founders navigating the pre-seed to Series A landscape, the excitement of receiving a term sheet can often cloud the dangerous reality of what those terms actually mean for your future.

Many founders make the fatal mistake of focusing solely on the valuation number while ignoring the structural mechanics buried in the clauses below. A high valuation with toxic terms is often worse than a lower valuation with clean terms. The difference between a successful exit and a founder being pushed out of their own company frequently comes down to how well you understood the **term sheet red flags** before you signed.

In Australia, where the investment ecosystem is maturing rapidly under the guidance of bodies like ASIC and the ATO, the sophistication of investors is increasing. However, not all term sheets are created equal. Some contain **bad term sheet clauses** that can strip you of control, destroy your upside, or make future fundraising impossible.

> **Key Insight:** According to data from the Australian Private Equity & Venture Capital Association (AVCAL), founder-friendly term sheets are becoming the standard, yet a significant portion of early-stage deals still include clauses that disproportionately favour the investor at the expense of long-term founder incentives.

You need to walk into your next negotiation with a clear understanding of the leverage you hold and the traps you must avoid. Let's dissect the ten most dangerous clauses you will encounter in an **investor term sheet guide**.

## 1. Liquidation Preferences: The 1X Standard vs. The Trap

Liquidation preference determines who gets paid first and how much when the company exits, whether through a sale or liquidation. The **term sheet red flags** here are often subtle but devastating.

The industry standard for early-stage deals is a **1x non-participating** liquidation preference. This means the investor gets their money back first, and then the remaining proceeds are shared pro-rata based on ownership. It is a fair mechanism that protects the investor's downside while allowing founders to share in the upside.

However, watch out for **2x or higher** liquidation preferences. This is a massive red flag. If a VC invests $1 million with a 2x preference, they get $2 million back before you see a single cent, regardless of your valuation at exit.

Even worse is the **participating liquidation preference**. In this scenario, the investor gets their initial money back *and* then participates in the remaining distribution as if they still owned their percentage. This effectively doubles (or triples) their payout at the expense of the founders and employees.

### The Impact on Your Exit

Consider a scenario where you sell your company for $10 million. You have $2 million in preferred shares.
*   **Standard 1x Non-Participating:** Investor gets $2M. You and the pool split the remaining $8M.
*   **2x Participating:** Investor gets $4M (2x). Then they take another share of the remaining $6M based on their ownership. Your slice of the pie shrinks drastically.

## 2. Full Ratchet Anti-Dilution: The Down Round Killer

Anti-dilution provisions protect investors if the company raises money at a lower valuation in the future (a "down round"). The mechanism is designed to adjust their conversion price so they own more shares, compensating for the drop in value.

There are two main types: **Weighted Average** and **Full Ratchet**.

**Weighted Average** is the standard, founder-friendly approach. It adjusts the conversion price based on the new lower price and the amount of money raised, providing a moderate protection for the investor.

**Full Ratchet**, however, is a **bad term sheet clause** that should be immediately rejected. Under a full ratchet, if the company raises a down round at any price, the investor's conversion price is adjusted to match that new, lower price, regardless of how little money is raised.

This clause can be catastrophic. If you raise a small bridge round at a slightly lower valuation, a full ratchet could wipe out the founder's equity overnight, diluting you so severely that you lose motivation to continue building the business.

| Provision Type | Mechanism | Founder Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Broad Weighted Average** | Adjusts price based on new shares and price. Moderate dilution. | **Low/Moderate** (Standard) |
| **Narrow Weighted Average** | Adjusts price but excludes certain preferred shares. Higher dilution. | **Moderate** |
| **Full Ratchet** | Adjusts price to the lowest new share price immediately. | **Catastrophic** (Red Flag) |

## 3. Board Control: Losing Your Steering Wheel

The board of directors holds the fiduciary power to hire, fire, and set strategy. In the early days, founders usually hold the majority of board seats. However, sophisticated investors will demand board control or significant influence.

A term sheet that grants the investor **control of the board** (a 2-1 split where the investor has two seats to your one, or a chairperson tie-breaking vote) is a significant **term sheet red flag**. This effectively puts the founder in the position of an employee with limited autonomy.

In the Australian context, under **Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)**, the board has specific legal duties. If an investor controls the board, they assume these duties, but it also means they can vote you out of your own company if they disagree with the direction, even if you are the majority shareholder.

Look for "protective provisions" that require investor consent for specific actions like hiring a CEO, changing the business model, or raising further capital. While some vetting is reasonable, giving a single investor veto power over day-to-day operations is a recipe for deadlock.

## 4. Drag-Along Rights: Forced Exits at Low Valuations

**Drag-along rights** allow a majority of shareholders to force minority shareholders to join in the sale of a company. This is a standard mechanism to ensure a single buyer can acquire 100% of the company without being held hostage by a small minority.

The red flag arises when the **threshold** is set too low or the **price floor** is non-existent. If a single investor (e.g., with 20% equity) can drag you along to sell the company at a price you believe is undervalued, you have lost control of your destiny.

In Australia, where exit events can sometimes be driven by strategic acquirers looking for quick consolidation, a poorly drafted drag-along clause can force a "fire sale" before you have hit your milestones.

Ensure the clause requires a super-majority vote (e.g., 75% or more) to trigger a drag-along. Furthermore, the clause should include a **minimum price floor** that you and your co-founders agree upon, preventing a low-ball offer from being forced through.

## 5. Pay-to-Play Provisions: The "Use It or Lose It" Trap

A **pay-to-play** clause requires existing investors to participate in future funding rounds to maintain their protective rights (like anti-dilution protection).

On the surface, this sounds reasonable—it ensures investors are committed. However, if structured poorly, it can be a trap. If an investor misses a round, they might lose their anti-dilution rights or even their board seat.

While this is often a **investor term sheet guide** favourite for sophisticated funds, for early-stage founders, it can create a hostile environment. If your lead investor decides not to participate in a bridge round due to internal fund constraints, a strict pay-to-play clause might strip them of their protections, potentially triggering a change of control or valuation adjustments that destabilise the cap table.

Ensure that "failure to pay" results in a proportionate reduction of rights, not a total loss of status, unless the investor completely refuses to support the company's survival.

## 6. Redemption Rights: The Loan That Wants to be Repaid

**Redemption rights** (or redemption provisions) give investors the right to force the company to buy back their shares after a certain period (usually 5-7 years) if an exit hasn't occurred.

This transforms an equity investment into a de facto loan. If your startup is a "zombie"—growing slowly but not exiting—this clause can bankrupt the company. You might have to use all your operating cash to redeem shares, killing your ability to pay staff or invest in growth.

Under **ATO** guidelines and standard Australian corporate law, redemption clauses can create complex tax implications if the company is forced to redeem shares at a premium.

> **Critical Statistic:** 40% of Australian startups fail to achieve a liquidity event within 7 years. If your term sheet includes strict redemption rights, you are risking solvency on an unpredictable timeline. Never accept a redemption clause that requires the company to pay a premium (e.g., 1x or 2x the original investment) if the company is not profitable. This is a classic **bad term sheet clause** that can force a distressed sale.

## 7. Super Pro-Rata Rights: Crowding Out Future Growth

Pro-rata rights allow investors to maintain their percentage ownership in future rounds by investing more money. This is generally standard and beneficial for the company as it shows investor confidence.

However, **super pro-rata rights** are a red flag. This clause allows an investor to buy *more* than their pro-rata share in future rounds, potentially diluting other shareholders (including founders) disproportionately.

If a lead investor has super pro-rata rights, they can effectively "crowd out" new investors or founders in a subsequent round. They can increase their stake to 30% or 40% if they choose, altering the balance of power and the cap table structure in a way that makes the company less attractive to future investors.

In the Australian ecosystem, where the investor pool is smaller than in the US, a single investor holding a super-majority position can create a deadlock that prevents future fundraising. Limit these rights to standard pro-rata maintenance only.

## 8. Information Rights: The Burden of Reporting

Investors need visibility into the company's performance. Standard **information rights** usually include monthly or quarterly financial reports and a board seat.

The red flag appears when these rights are excessive, requiring weekly detailed reports, direct access to all employees, or audits on a schedule that disrupts operations. This creates an administrative burden that distracts the founding team.

Under **ASIC** regulations, companies must maintain certain records, but adding contractual obligations for hourly revenue tracking or deep-dive employee interviews goes beyond standard governance.

Ensure that information rights are limited to standard financial statements (P&L, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow) and high-level KPIs delivered on a quarterly basis.

## 9. No-Shop and Exclusivity: The Time Trap

A **no-shop** clause prevents you from talking to other investors for a set period after signing the term sheet. This is standard to protect the investor's time and due diligence costs.

However, the **duration** is the issue. A 60 or 90-day exclusivity period is a massive **term sheet red flag** for an early-stage company. Startups move fast, and missing a funding window can be fatal.

If the investor drags out their due diligence for 60 days while you are unable to talk to competitors, you risk running out of cash. The standard in Australia is a **30-day** exclusivity period, with a clear deadline for closing the deal. If they miss the deadline, the no-shop clause must expire immediately.

## 10. Founder Vesting and Re-Vesting: The Loyalty Bond

Founders are often asked to vest their shares over time, which is standard. However, **re-vesting** clauses are a trap.

Re-vesting requires founders who have already been working for the company for years to start a *new* vesting schedule upon raising a new round. This implies that your past work has no value and that you are essentially a new hire.

This is a psychological blow and a financial trap. It can result in founders leaving the company with significantly less equity than they originally earned.

> **Expert View:** "Re-vesting is a signal that the investor does not trust the founder's commitment. In healthy Australian deals, founders' existing equity is grandfathered, and only new options are subject to vesting." – *Founder Advocate, Melbourne VC Circle*

Ensure that your existing shares are fully vested or vested according to the original schedule, and any new vesting only applies to *new* grants.

## The Founder's Due Diligence Checklist

Before you sign a term sheet, run through this checklist to ensure you haven't walked into a trap. This is your first line of defense against **bad term sheet clauses**.

- [ ] **Liquidation Preference:** Is it 1x non-participating? (Reject anything higher or participating).
- [ ] **Anti-Dilution:** Is it Broad Weighted Average? (Reject Full Ratchet immediately).
- [ ] **Board Composition:** Do founders retain a majority or at least an equal split with a founder-friendly chair?
- [ ] **Drag-Along:** Is the threshold at least 75% with a minimum price floor?
- [ ] **Redemption:** Is there a redemption clause? If yes, is it strictly limited to solvent events? (Ideally, remove it).
- [ ] **Pro-Rata:** Are rights limited to maintaining current ownership? (Reject Super Pro-Rata).
- [ ] **Information Rights:** Are they limited to quarterly financials?
- [ ] **No-Shop:** Is the exclusivity period limited to 30 days?
- [ ] **Founder Vesting:** Is there any re-vesting clause? (Reject immediately).
- [ ] **Legal Review:** Has an Australian startup lawyer reviewed the full legal documents, not just the term sheet?

## Why Valuation Isn't Everything

It is tempting to chase the highest pre-money valuation, but a high number with toxic terms is a pyrrhic victory. A **startup term sheet** that strips you of control, dilutes you to zero in a down round, or forces an early exit is a dead end.

In the Australian market, the focus is shifting towards sustainable growth and founder-friendly terms. Investors are increasingly aware that empowering founders leads to higher returns. However, you must still be vigilant.

Your **Startup Value Index (SVI)** is a reflection of your company's health, but your term sheet determines how much of that value you actually get to keep. Understanding these clauses is not just about legal compliance; it is about strategic positioning for your next growth phase.

The most successful founders are those who negotiate not just for cash, but for the freedom to execute their vision without restrictive shackles. By identifying and rejecting **term sheet red flags**, you protect your company's future and ensure that when you do exit, the reward is shared fairly.
