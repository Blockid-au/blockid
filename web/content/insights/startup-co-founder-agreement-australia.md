## Co-Founder Agreement Template for Australian Startups: What to Include

Co-founder disputes are one of the top three causes of early startup failure in Australia. The founding team dynamic that felt natural over coffee and a shared whiteboard can fracture spectacularly under the pressure of building a company — and when it does, the legal and financial consequences can be catastrophic if the right documents are not in place.

A co-founder agreement is not a sign of distrust. It is the operational manual for your most important business relationship. Done well, it eliminates the most common sources of conflict before they arise, establishes clear expectations on contribution and equity, and protects all founders — including you — from scenarios you cannot yet imagine.

This guide covers everything an Australian co-founder agreement needs to include, the key decisions you must make before signing, and the legal framework under which it operates.

> **Key Insight:** According to data from Startup Genome, co-founder conflict is cited in approximately 23% of startup failures. In Australia, where the legal costs of resolving equity disputes can exceed $100,000, a well-drafted co-founder agreement is the highest-ROI legal document you will ever create.

## Why a Verbal Agreement Is Not Enough

Many founding teams operate for months on handshake agreements and good intentions. "We'll sort out the legal stuff when we raise." This is a dangerous gamble for three reasons:

**1. Memory diverges under pressure.** What both founders "agreed" about the 50/50 split at the kitchen table may be remembered very differently when one is working 80-hour weeks and the other has moved to part-time.

**2. Verbal agreements are unenforceable for equity matters.** Under Australian contract law and the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), equity ownership in a company is governed by the share register — which records legal title. A verbal promise to give someone equity is not enforceable against the company's share register without a written agreement.

**3. ASIC and investors require written records.** Any investor conducting due diligence will ask for the co-founder agreement. "We have a verbal arrangement" will end the conversation.

## What a Co-Founder Agreement Must Cover

### 1. Equity Allocation and Vesting

The equity split is the first and most contentious conversation. There is no universally correct answer, but there is a universal best practice: **all founder equity should be subject to a vesting schedule**.

Vesting protects every founder — including you. If your co-founder leaves after six months with no vesting, they walk away with their full equity allocation and you are left building the company alone while they hold a 40% stake. Vesting creates a mechanism where equity is earned over time based on continued contribution.

**Standard Australian co-founder vesting terms:**
- 4-year total vesting period
- 1-year cliff (nothing vests in the first 12 months; at month 12, 25% vests)
- Monthly vesting thereafter (2.083% per month for the remaining 36 months)
- Acceleration provisions on double trigger (change of control + termination)

The equity split itself should reflect the contributions being made — not just at inception, but on a forward-looking basis. Factors to weigh include: the value of the idea (low), the value of prior-built IP (medium), execution capability (high), commitment level (high), and market access (medium).

### Equity Allocation Framework

| Founder Contribution | Weight | Example Scenario |
|---|---|---|
| **Full-time commitment, CEO role** | 40–50% | Founder A: 45% |
| **Full-time, CTO, built MVP** | 35–45% | Founder B: 40% |
| **Part-time, domain expertise** | 10–20% | Founder C: 15% |
| **Advisor with equity** | 0.25–2% | Separate advisor agreement |
| **ESOP pool (pre-raise)** | 10–15% | Reserved for future hires |

### 2. Roles, Responsibilities, and Decision Rights

Ambiguity about who decides what is the operational cause of most co-founder disputes. The agreement must clearly define:

- **Executive roles:** CEO, CTO, CPO, COO — who holds each and what it means in practice
- **Domain authority:** Which decisions each founder owns without needing the other's consent (e.g., CEO owns sales and fundraising; CTO owns engineering and architecture)
- **Shared decision threshold:** Decisions that require both founders' agreement (e.g., hiring above a salary threshold, raising capital, pivoting the business, issuing new shares)
- **Dispute resolution process:** How you resolve disagreements on shared decisions (mediation before litigation)

### 3. Good Leaver and Bad Leaver Provisions

One of the most important — and most negotiated — clauses in any co-founder agreement is the "good leaver / bad leaver" provision. This determines what happens to a departing founder's unvested equity.

**Good leaver:** A founder who leaves for reasons beyond their control (illness, death, redundancy, or mutual agreement). Good leavers typically retain their vested equity and may receive accelerated partial vesting of unvested equity as a goodwill gesture.

**Bad leaver:** A founder who resigns voluntarily, is terminated for cause (gross misconduct, material breach, competitive activity), or breaches the co-founder agreement. Bad leavers typically forfeit all unvested equity and, in some cases, face a buyback of vested equity at a reduced price (e.g., the lower of cost price or fair market value).

Under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), buyback provisions must be carefully drafted to comply with share buyback rules. Engage an Australian startup lawyer to ensure the mechanics are enforceable.

### 4. Intellectual Property Assignment

The co-founder agreement must include a clear IP assignment clause, transferring all IP created before and during the co-founders' work on the company to the company entity. This complements a standalone IP Assignment Agreement (which should also be signed) by creating a contractual obligation between the co-founders themselves.

This is particularly critical for the technical co-founder who may have built a prototype, written code, or developed algorithms before the company was formally incorporated. Without a written assignment, the company does not legally own that work.

### 5. Non-Compete and Non-Solicit Restrictions

Australian courts are generally reluctant to enforce overly broad non-compete clauses. For a non-compete to be enforceable, it must:

- Be **reasonable in scope** (limited to specific activities, not an entire industry)
- Be **reasonable in duration** (typically 12–24 months; anything longer is rarely enforced)
- Be **reasonable in geography** (limited to markets where the company operates)
- Protect a **legitimate business interest** (not merely restricting competition generally)

A typical enforceable clause for an Australian co-founder would restrict them from directly competing in the same product category, in the same geographic market, for 12–18 months post-departure.

### 6. Salary and Remuneration

Many early-stage founders take no salary or a minimal salary in the pre-revenue phase. The co-founder agreement should:

- Document the initial agreed salary (or zero salary) to avoid future disputes about what was "owed"
- Set a clear trigger for when salary reviews occur (first revenue milestone, first external funding)
- Establish how salary changes are approved (e.g., both founders plus an independent director's sign-off)

## The Relationship Between a Co-Founder Agreement and a Shareholders Agreement

A co-founder agreement is typically signed before or at incorporation, when there are no external investors. It governs the relationship between the founders specifically.

A shareholders agreement (SHA) is a broader document that governs the relationship between all shareholders — including future investors. When you raise external capital, your investors will require a SHA that supersedes or supplements the co-founder agreement.

Best practice is to structure your co-founder agreement so it converts seamlessly into a shareholders agreement, or is explicitly superseded by one at the time of your first external investment.

## Common Co-Founder Agreement Mistakes in Australia

- **Equal split without vesting:** A 50/50 split with no vesting schedule is the most dangerous equity structure for any startup. If one founder leaves in month three, they retain half the company.
- **No buyback mechanism:** Without a buyback provision, a departed founder's equity is permanently locked out of future reallocation. You cannot easily bring on new talent without diluting everyone.
- **Vague roles:** "We'll both do everything" is not a role definition. It guarantees overlap, conflict, and unclear accountability.
- **Ignoring the non-cash contributions:** IP, customer relationships, domain expertise, and network have real value. The equity split should reflect all contributions, not just time.

## Conclusion: Sign Before You Build

The best time to sign a co-founder agreement is before any meaningful work begins and before incorporation. The second-best time is right now.

The conversations required to complete this agreement — about equity, roles, departure scenarios, and IP — are uncomfortable. That discomfort is exactly why you need to have them. Any co-founder relationship that cannot survive an honest conversation about equity and accountability at the beginning is unlikely to survive the real pressures of building a startup.

Have the conversation. Get the agreement drafted by an Australian startup lawyer. Sign it before you build anything valuable. Your future self — and your investors — will thank you.
