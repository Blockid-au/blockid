## SAFE Notes vs Convertible Notes: What Australian Founders Need to Know in 2026

The most consequential decision an early-stage Australian founder makes is not what to build — it is how to structure the first external investment. In 2026, that choice almost always comes down to two instruments: the **SAFE note** (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) and the **Convertible Note**. Both defer the valuation conversation to a later priced round. But their legal structures, tax implications, and strategic consequences are fundamentally different.

Choosing the wrong instrument can saddle your startup with debt obligations at the worst possible time, create unexpected tax liabilities for founders and investors, or create a cap table mess that derails your Series A. This guide covers the practical differences Australian founders need to understand before they sign anything.

> **2026 Market Data:** SAFEs now represent **62% of Australian pre-seed deals**, up from 55% in 2023. However, Convertible Notes remain dominant for raises above $500K and for investors from family offices or high-net-worth networks where debt-like instruments are preferred.

## The Core Legal Difference: Debt vs Equity Agreement

This distinction is not semantic. It has direct consequences for your balance sheet, your obligations to investors, and your ability to raise future rounds.

**A SAFE is an equity agreement.** It is a promise to issue shares in the future, at a price determined by a future priced round. A SAFE is not a loan. It does not sit on your balance sheet as a liability. It accrues no interest. It has no maturity date. If you never raise a priced round, a SAFE investor has no mechanism to demand repayment.

**A Convertible Note is debt.** It is a loan that converts to equity upon a qualifying event (typically a priced round). It carries an interest rate (typically 4-8% per annum in Australia), a maturity date (typically 12-24 months), and a legal obligation to repay principal plus accrued interest if the note is not converted before maturity. If your startup stalls and you cannot raise a priced round before maturity, you face a debt obligation that can force insolvency.

| Feature | SAFE Note | Convertible Note |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Legal Nature** | Equity agreement | Debt instrument |
| **Balance Sheet** | Off balance sheet | Liability (debt) |
| **Interest Rate** | None | 4–8% p.a. |
| **Maturity Date** | None | 12–24 months |
| **Repayment Risk** | None (no maturity) | Yes, if not converted |
| **Legal Cost (AUD)** | $1,500 – $4,000 | $4,000 – $12,000 |
| **Valuation Cap** | Standard | Standard |
| **Discount Rate** | Standard (15-25%) | Standard (15-25%) |
| **ASIC Requirements** | Disclosure document may apply | Regulated as a debenture |
| **ATO Treatment** | No immediate income event | Interest is deductible (company) |
| **Best For** | Speed, simplicity, pre-seed | Conservative investors, larger raises |

## The Australian Regulatory Layer

This is where Australian founders diverge from the US playbook. Standard YC SAFE documents are written under Delaware law and reference US-specific legal concepts. Using an un-localised US SAFE in Australia creates three specific risks:

**1. ASIC disclosure obligations.** Under the *Corporations Act 2001* (Cth), issuing securities (which includes SAFE instruments) typically requires a disclosure document (prospectus or information memorandum). However, small-scale personal offer exemptions apply for offers to fewer than 20 investors and under $2M in a 12-month period. Most early-stage Australian SAFEs are structured to fall within this exemption. Founders must count their investors carefully.

**2. Pre-emptive rights confusion.** Australian corporate law, and most standard shareholder agreements, grant existing shareholders pre-emptive rights on new share issuances. A SAFE that converts to shares without triggering a pre-emption right process can void the issuance. Your lawyer must address this explicitly.

**3. ATO tax treatment at conversion.** When a SAFE converts to shares, the ATO may treat the shares as an employee share scheme benefit if the SAFE holder is also a founder or employee. This can trigger an immediate income tax event. This is rare for arm's-length investor SAFEs but critical to understand for founder-investor hybrid structures.

**Recommendation:** Use an Australian SAFE template from Australasian Equity Crowdfunding or a local startup law firm (Gilbert + Tobin, Piper Alderman, or Hall & Wilcox all have standard AU-localised SAFE documents). Expect to pay $1,500 – $3,500 for a properly localised document.

## How Valuation Caps Work in Practice

Both SAFEs and Convertible Notes typically include a **valuation cap** — the maximum valuation at which the instrument will convert to equity. This protects early investors from being diluted if the startup's valuation skyrockets before the priced round.

**Example:** You raise $200K on a SAFE with a $4M valuation cap. Six months later, you raise a $3M seed round at a $12M pre-money valuation.

- Without a cap: your SAFE investor's $200K converts at $12M, giving them 1.67% of the company.
- With a $4M cap: your SAFE investor's $200K converts at $4M, giving them 5% of the company.

The cap is a critical negotiating lever. **Australian market data for 2026 shows:**

| Stage | Typical Cap Range (AUD) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Pre-seed (idea stage) | $2M – $4M |
| Pre-seed (MVP, some users) | $3.5M – $6M |
| Seed bridge | $6M – $12M |

## Discount Rates: The Other Lever

A discount rate (typically 15-25%) gives early investors a lower share price than subsequent investors at the priced round — rewarding them for taking early risk.

**Example:** Seed round priced at $1.00/share. SAFE investor has a 20% discount. SAFE converts at $0.80/share, giving the investor 25% more shares than if they had invested at the seed round price.

In Australia, discount rates of 20% are standard for pre-seed SAFEs and Convertible Notes raised 12+ months before a priced round. For bridge notes raised close to a priced round (within 3-6 months), 15% is more typical.

**Important:** SAFEs and Convertible Notes can have both a cap and a discount. At conversion, investors receive whichever gives them the better price (lower share price). This is called a "most-favoured nation" mechanism and is standard in Australian SAFE templates.

## The 2026 Stacking Problem

One of the most damaging patterns in Australian early-stage cap tables is **SAFE stacking** — issuing multiple SAFEs over 18-24 months with different caps, different discount rates, and no clear timeline to a priced round.

By the time a founder reaches seed, they may have 8-12 SAFE investors with different economic terms, creating a complex conversion calculation and a cap table that institutional VCs find difficult to underwrite. In 2026, several high-profile Australian Series A deals stalled due to SAFE stacking issues.

**Best practice:** Limit pre-seed SAFEs to a single round with a single set of terms. If you need a bridge, issue a second SAFE only when you are 3-4 months from a priced round, and keep all terms identical to the prior SAFE.

## When Should You Use a Convertible Note Instead?

Despite the simplicity advantages of SAFEs, Convertible Notes remain the right tool in specific situations:

1. **Investor preference.** Family offices and high-net-worth individuals accustomed to debt instruments often require a Convertible Note structure. In 2026, roughly 70% of Australian family office investments at pre-seed are structured as Convertible Notes.

2. **Larger raises.** For raises above $750K at pre-seed, many Australian sophisticated investors require a Convertible Note because the debt characterisation gives them stronger protections (creditor ranking, formal maturity, accrued interest as additional upside).

3. **ESIC eligibility planning.** Convertible Notes may be more straightforward for ESIC eligibility because they are treated as issued securities (debentures) at the time of investment, potentially locking in the ESIC benefits sooner.

4. **Foreign investors.** US and UK angel investors may prefer Convertible Notes if they have tax reasons to prefer debt instruments in Australian entities.

## The Decision Framework

Use this framework to choose between a SAFE and a Convertible Note for your Australian raise:

```
Raise under $500K AND investors are angels/accelerators
→ Use a SAFE (localised AU template)

Raise $500K–$1.5M AND investors are angels or family offices
→ Negotiate: SAFE if investors are comfortable, Convertible Note if they prefer debt

Raise above $1.5M at pre-seed
→ Consider a priced round (SAFE stacking risk becomes significant above this level)

Investors are family offices / HNWs preferring debt instruments
→ Use a Convertible Note

Investor is international (US / UK)
→ SAFE (localised), confirm ASIC exemption applies
```
