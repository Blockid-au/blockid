## Why Your Cap Table Matters More Than You Think

You've got a brilliant idea, you're assembling your founding team, and someone asks: "What's my equity stake?" If you're scrambling to figure out the answer, you're not alone. Most Australian founders operate on handshake agreements and vague promises during the early days. But this casual approach can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars and create legal chaos when you need to raise capital or bring on investors.

Your cap table (capitalisation table) is the single source of truth for who owns what in your company. It tracks every share issued, option, warrant, and convertible security. Without it, you're essentially running blind—and investors will spot the problem immediately.

This guide walks you through building a proper cap table as an Australian startup. We'll cover ASIC compliance requirements, the difference between ordinary and preference shares, ESOP pools, vesting schedules, and common mistakes that sink founder relationships and funding rounds.

## What Is a Cap Table and Why Australian Startups Need One

A cap table is a spreadsheet (or database) that shows the complete ownership structure of your company. It lists:

- **Founders** and their shareholdings
- **Employees** with share options or equity grants
- **Investors** and their preference share holdings
- **Convertible securities** like SAFEs or convertible notes
- **Option pools** reserved for future hires
- **Share class details** (ordinary vs preference shares)

In Australia, you're required to maintain an accurate share register under the *Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)*. ASIC expects this register to be updated within certain timeframes—especially after issuing new shares or granting options. Getting this wrong creates compliance headaches and can cloud your title to the company.

More practically, your cap table answers critical questions:

- How diluted will my founder stake become after a $2M Series A?
- What's the true value of my employee's option grant?
- Who controls board seats and voting rights?
- What happens to options if we're acquired?

Without clarity here, you'll lose credibility with investors, struggle to attract talent, and risk founder disputes that derail your business.

## Australian Regulatory Requirements: ASIC and Tax Compliance

Before you build your cap table, understand the Australian regulatory landscape.

### ASIC Share Register Requirements

ASIC requires Australian companies to maintain a share register that records:

- The name and address of each member (shareholder)
- The number of shares held by each member
- The date shares were issued
- The share class and whether shares are fully or partly paid

You must update this register **within two months** of any share issue or transfer. If you're a proprietary company (which most startups are), you can maintain a simple register—but it must be accurate and accessible to members and ASIC on demand.

**Common mistake:** Many founders keep equity promises in emails or Slack messages. ASIC doesn't recognise these. You need formal share certificates or a statutory declaration, signed by directors, confirming the share transfer.

### ATO and Taxation of Equity

The Australian Taxation Office treats share issuance and options differently depending on how you structure them:

- **Ordinary shares issued to founders at par value** (often $1 per share) typically don't trigger capital gains tax for the founder at grant time.
- **Employee share schemes** (like options) may qualify for concessional tax treatment under Division 83A-CA of the *Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (Cth)*, but only if the scheme meets specific requirements. Consult your accountant on this.
- **Preference shares** issued to investors are treated as issued capital and don't usually trigger immediate tax, but they carry different rights and restrictions.

The key point: get your accountant involved early. Structuring equity incorrectly can trigger unexpected tax bills for founders and employees.

### ESIC and Workers' Compensation

If you're issuing equity to employees, ensure your workers' compensation insurer is aware. Some policies exclude equity-holding employees from coverage under certain conditions. Check your policy and notify ESIC (in your state) if needed.

## Building Your Cap Table: Step-by-Step

### Step 1: Define Your Share Capital

Start by deciding how much authorised share capital your company has. Most Australian startups use simple structures:

- **$1 million in authorised ordinary shares** (at $0.001 per share, giving you flexibility)
- A small option pool reserved for employees (typically 10-20% of fully diluted equity)

You'll set this when you incorporate, and you can increase it later if needed (requires a shareholder resolution).

**Example:**
- Authorised capital: 1,000,000 ordinary shares
- Issued to founders: 400,000 shares (40% each to two co-founders)
- Reserved for ESOP pool: 200,000 shares
- Remaining: 400,000 shares unissued (for future investment rounds)

### Step 2: Issue Shares to Founders

Create a formal **Founder Share Certificate** for each co-founder. This document should specify:

- Shareholder name and ACN
- Number of shares issued
- Share class (ordinary)
- Issue date
- Consideration paid (even if nominal, e.g., $1)
- Any vesting conditions (see vesting section below)

**Template excerpt:**
```
SHARE CERTIFICATE

Company: [Your Startup Name] ACN [ACN]
Shareholder: [Founder Name]
Number of Shares: [X] ordinary shares
Share Class: Ordinary
Issue Date: [DD/MM/YYYY]
Consideration: $[amount]

These shares are issued subject to the Constitution and [any shareholder agreement].
```

File a copy with your company secretary (or in your corporate records if you're a microcompany). Update your ASIC share register immediately.

### Step 3: Establish Your Option Pool and ESOP Framework

An option pool reserves a percentage of shares for future employee grants. The typical range for Australian startups is 10-20% of fully diluted equity. This gives you room to offer competitive equity packages without diluting founders excessively.

**How it works:**

1. Board approves an Employee Share Option Plan (ESOP) that sets out:
   - Total pool size (e.g., 100,000 options)
   - Exercise price (usually the share price at the time of grant, or par value)
   - Vesting schedule (see next section)
   - Leaver provisions (what happens if the employee departs)
   - Acceleration provisions (if there's a change of control)

2. When you hire an employee, you grant them options from this pool under the ESOP. They don't own the shares yet—they own the right to purchase shares at a fixed price.

3. The options vest over time (typically 4 years), incentivizing the employee to stay.

**Why options instead of shares?** Options preserve the founder stake and allow tax-concessional treatment for employees under Division 83A-CA (if structured correctly). They also create a performance incentive: the employee only benefits if the company is worth more at their exercise date than the strike price.

### Step 4: Set Up Vesting Schedules

Vesting ensures founders and employees are incentivized to stay. The standard Australian tech startup structure is **4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff**:

- Employee receives 0% of their equity in the first 12 months (the "cliff").
- On the 1-year anniversary, they vest 25% immediately.
- After that, they vest 1/48th of the total grant each month for the remaining 36 months.

**Formula:** Monthly vesting = Total grant ÷ 48 months

**Example:**
An employee is granted 48,000 options with a 4-year vesting schedule and 1-year cliff.
- At month 12: 12,000 options vest (25%)
- At month 13: additional 1,000 options vest (1/48th)
- At month 24: 24,000 total options have vested (50%)
- At month 48: 48,000 options have vested (100%)

**Founder vesting:** Many investors expect founders to be on a vesting schedule too. This protects the company (and co-founders) if a founder exits early. A common approach is a 4-year schedule with a 1-year cliff, but sometimes the cliff is shorter or absent if the founder is fully committed from day one.

Include vesting terms in your cap table alongside each shareholder or option holder.

## Ordinary Shares vs. Preference Shares: What Australian Startups Need to Know

Most founder equity is issued as **ordinary shares**. Investors typically receive **preference shares** with special rights.

### Ordinary Shares

- Voting rights (typically 1 vote per share)
- Right to dividends (if declared)
- Right to the residual assets if the company is liquidated (after debt is paid)
- No preferential treatment

### Preference Shares

Investors negotiate preference share terms that protect their downside:

- **Liquidation preference** (e.g., "1x non-participating"): The investor gets their money back first if the company is sold or wound up, but doesn't participate in additional proceeds. A "2x" means they get 2x their investment back, and so on.
- **Anti-dilution rights**: If the company raises capital at a lower valuation, the investor's share count is adjusted upward to protect their stake.
- **Dividend rights**: Some preference shares carry a preferential dividend (e.g., 8% p.a.), paid before ordinary dividends.
- **Redemption rights**: The investor can force the company to buy back their shares under certain conditions.
- **Board seat or observer rights**: Investors often negotiate board representation.
- **Drag-along and tag-along rights**: These control how the company can be sold. Drag-along lets majority shareholders force minorities to sell. Tag-along lets minorities sell their shares on the same terms as the majority.

**For your cap table:** Track preference shares separately. Record the liquidation preference, anti-dilution type, and key terms. This affects how you calculate fully diluted ownership and what outcomes each class of shareholder can expect.

## Common Cap Table Mistakes That Cost Founders Money

### Mistake 1: Informal Equity Promises

**The problem:** A co-founder or early employee believes they're getting equity based on a conversation, but there's no paper trail. If a dispute arises, there's nothing to prove the promise.

**The fix:**
- Document every equity grant in writing, signed by the board or all shareholders.
- Include the number of shares, exercise price (if options), vesting schedule, and any conditions.
- Keep signed documents in a corporate folder, accessible to all shareholders.

### Mistake 2: Missing Shareholder Agreement

**The problem:** Multiple shareholders with no agreement specifying their rights creates chaos. Who can sell? Who decides strategy? What if a founder wants to exit?

**The fix:**
- Draft a **Shareholders' Agreement** early, even if it's simple.
- Cover: information rights (who gets to see financial statements?), tag-along/drag-along rights, pre-emptive rights (can other shareholders buy if someone tries to sell?), and deadlock resolution.
- Have all shareholders sign. Update it when new investors join.

### Mistake 3: Incorrect Option Exercise Price

**The problem:** If options are granted at a below-market strike price, the employee may face unexpected tax bills. If options are granted above fair market value, employees feel ripped off and the options are worthless.

**The fix:**
- Have your company formally valued (even a simple 409A-equivalent valuation) when issuing options.
- Set the strike price at fair market value as of the grant date.
- Document the valuation in board minutes.

### Mistake 4: Not Tracking Leaver Provisions

**The problem:** An employee leaves, and you have no agreed mechanism to handle their vested or unvested options. Do they have 90 days to exercise? Does the company buy back unvested options?

**The fix:**
- Define leaver provisions in your ESOP:
  - **Good leaver:** Vested options can be exercised for 5-10 years (or longer).
  - **Bad leaver:** Vested options can be exercised for 30-90 days; unvested options are forfeited.
- Distinguish between resignation, termination for cause, and termination without cause.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Dilution in Future Rounds

**The problem:** A founder thinks they own 50% of the company, but doesn't account for the dilution from future investment rounds. After Series A, their stake drops to 30%, and they're shocked.

**The fix:**
- Build a simple model showing founder ownership post-Series A, Series B, etc.
- Use a cap table calculator (see below) to run scenarios.
- Discuss dilution expectations with co-founders and investors upfront. This prevents disputes later.

**What each column means:**

- **Shareholder Name:** Full legal name or entity name
- **Share Class:** Ordinary, Preference A, Preference B, or Option

- **Vesting Start/End:** First day and last day of vesting period
- **Vesting %age:** How much has vested as of today
- **Notes:** Key terms (preference share rights, cliff, leaver provisions, etc.)

## Managing Your Cap Table Over Time

As you grow, your cap table becomes more complex. Here's what to track:

### After Each Funding Round

- Update share counts for new investor equity (or converted convertible notes).
- Adjust for any anti-dilution adjustments from previous investors.
- Record preference share terms (liquidation preference, anti-dilution type, board rights).
- File updated share register with ASIC if required.

### When Issuing Options

- Record the grant date, number of options, strike price, and vesting schedule.
- Ensure strike price is documented as fair market value (get a valuation if substantial grants are being made).
- Have all shareholders receive notice (some agreements require shareholder approval for large pools).

### On Employee Departure

- Calculate vested vs. unvested options.
- Apply leaver provisions and communicate timelines (e.g., 90 days to exercise for a bad leaver).
- Update cap table to reflect option expiration or buyback.

### Before Acquisition or IPO

- Prepare a detailed, audited cap table showing all shareholders, share classes, preferences, and fully diluted ownership.
- This document is mission-critical for due diligence. Any errors can derail or delay a deal.
