## Cap Table Red Flags Australia: Avoid Common Startup Mistakes

As an Australian startup founder, you're likely no stranger to the challenges of managing your company's cap table. A well-maintained cap table is crucial for attracting investors, but a poorly managed one can be a major turn-off. In this article, we'll explore the 8 common cap table issues that can make VCs walk away and provide actionable advice on how to fix them before fundraising.

### The Importance of a Clean Cap Table

A clean cap table is essential for any startup looking to raise funds. It provides a clear picture of your company's ownership structure, which is critical for investors. A messy cap table, on the other hand, can raise red flags and deter potential investors.

> **"A clean cap table is a sign of a well-run company. It's a reflection of your attention to detail and your ability to manage complex issues."** - Anonymous VC

## Common Cap Table Red Flags in Australia

Here are the 8 common cap table issues that can make VCs walk away:

### 1. Too Many Small Angels

Having too many small angel investors can make your cap table look cluttered and difficult to manage. This can also lead to increased administrative burdens and costs.

| **Issue** | **Impact** | **Solution** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Too many small angels | Cluttered cap table, increased admin burden | Consolidate angel investors, set a minimum investment threshold |

### 2. Missing Vesting Schedules

Missing vesting schedules can lead to uncertainty and disputes over equity ownership. This is a major red flag for investors.

> **"Vesting schedules are essential for ensuring that founders and employees are committed to the long-term success of the company."**

### 3. Wrong Share Classes

Using the wrong share classes can lead to confusion and disputes over equity ownership. This is a common mistake that can be easily avoided.

| **Share Class** | **Description** | **Use Case** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ordinary shares | Represent ownership in the company | Founders, employees, investors |
| Preference shares | Have special rights and privileges | Investors, with liquidation preference |

### 4. Family Member Equity

Having family members on the cap table can raise concerns about nepotism and fairness.

> **"Family member equity can be a sensitive topic. It's essential to ensure that it's handled fairly and transparently."** 

### 5. Advisor Over-Allocation

Over-allocating equity to advisors can dilute founder ownership and create tension.

| **Advisor Equity Allocation** | **Industry Benchmark** | **Best Practice** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1-2% | 0.5-1% | Set a clear equity allocation policy |

### 6. Founder Loans Converting to Equity

Founder loans converting to equity can create complexity and uncertainty.

> **"Founder loans should be clearly documented and converted to equity on a predetermined schedule."** 

### 7. Missing IP Assignments

Missing IP assignments can put your company's intellectual property at risk.

| **IP Asset** | **Description** | **Importance** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Patents | Protect innovative products and services | High |
| Trademarks | Protect brand identity | Medium |
| Copyrights | Protect creative works | Low |

### 8. Share Class Errors

Share class errors can lead to confusion and disputes over equity ownership.

> **"Share class errors can be costly and time-consuming to fix. It's essential to get it right from the start."** 

## How to Fix Your Cap Table Before Fundraising

Fixing your cap table before fundraising is crucial for attracting investors. Here are some steps you can take:

- [ ] Review your cap table regularly
- [ ] Consolidate angel investors
- [ ] Implement vesting schedules
- [ ] Use correct share classes
- [ ] Document family member equity
- [ ] Set clear advisor equity allocation policies
- [ ] Document founder loans
- [ ] Assign IP assets
