## How to Value Your Startup in 2026: Methods, Benchmarks & Tools

Startup valuation in 2026 is part science, part negotiation, and part market timing. For Australian founders — operating in a smaller, more relationship-driven ecosystem than Silicon Valley — knowing which valuation method your investor is applying, and why, determines whether you can defend your number in a term sheet conversation or simply accept whatever you are offered.

This guide covers the four primary valuation methods used across the Australian startup ecosystem in 2026, AU-specific benchmarks by stage, the SVI framework developed by BlockID, and how to present your valuation in a way investors will respect.

## Why Valuation Methods Matter More Than the Number

Most founders obsess about the valuation number. Sophisticated founders obsess about which method the investor is using to arrive at that number — because that is what you can influence.

Each method has different inputs. If you know an investor is applying the Scorecard Method, you know that team strength carries 30% of the weight, and you prepare accordingly. If they are using the VC Method, you know they are working backwards from an exit scenario, and your job is to make the exit path credible and the comparable acquisitions concrete.

## Method 1: The Berkus Method

**Best for:** Pre-revenue startups, idea-to-MVP stage
**Used by:** Angel investors, family offices, early syndicates

The Berkus Method assigns a dollar value ceiling to each of five risk-reduction milestones. It was designed for pre-revenue startups where traditional financial modelling is meaningless. Scaled to AUD in 2026:

| Milestone Achieved | Maximum Value Added (AUD) |
| :--- | :---: |
| Sound idea (basic value) | A$500K |
| Prototype or working product | A$1M |
| Quality management team | A$1M |
| Strategic relationships (signed customers, partners) | A$1M |
| Product rollout or first revenue | A$1M |

**Maximum Berkus pre-revenue valuation: A$4.5M.** This aligns with the upper end of pre-seed valuations in the current Australian market, making Berkus a useful floor check. If your Berkus analysis gives you A$2M and you are pitching at A$5M, you need a compelling narrative about what makes your opportunity exceptional.

**Limitation:** Berkus ignores market size, competitive dynamics, and capital efficiency. Use it as a starting point, not a closing argument.

## Method 2: The Scorecard Method

**Best for:** Pre-seed and seed, with or without revenue
**Used by:** Angel investors, accelerators (Antler, Startmate), micro-VCs

The Scorecard Method starts with a regional baseline valuation — an average of recent comparable raises in your market — and adjusts it up or down based on five to seven weighted factors.

**2026 AU baseline valuations:**
- Pre-seed (no product, no revenue): A$1.5M–$2.5M
- Pre-revenue with MVP: A$2.5M–$4M
- Seed with early traction: A$4M–$7M

**Scorecard factors and weights (Australian adaptation):**

| Factor | Weight | Typical Range |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| Strength of founding team | 30% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Size of the opportunity (TAM) | 25% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Product / technology | 15% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Competitive environment | 10% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Marketing and sales channels | 10% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Need for additional investment | 5% | 0.5x–1.5x |
| Other (ESIC, governance, ESOP) | 5% | 0.5x–1.5x |

**Example:** A Melbourne B2B SaaS startup with two ex-Atlassian founders (1.4x on team), a clear wedge in a A$2B TAM (1.2x on market), and three signed LOIs (1.1x on traction) produces a composite multiplier of approximately 1.25x on a A$3.5M baseline — resulting in a pre-money valuation of A$4.4M.

## Method 3: The VC Method

**Best for:** Seed and Series A rounds with identifiable exit comparables
**Used by:** Blackbird, AirTree, Folklore, Square Peg, institutional VCs generally

The VC Method works backwards from an expected exit. The logic: a VC needs to know what return multiple they can achieve from their entry valuation, and they estimate this by projecting your exit value and discounting it by their required return.

**The formula:**
```
Post-Money Valuation = Projected Exit Value ÷ Required Return Multiple
Pre-Money Valuation = Post-Money Valuation − Investment Amount
```

**AU B2B SaaS seed example:**
- Projected exit in 5 years: A$60M (10x revenue multiple on A$6M ARR)
- Required return for seed fund: 20x
- Implied post-money: A$60M ÷ 20 = A$3M
- Investment: A$1.5M
- Pre-money: A$3M − A$1.5M = **A$1.5M**

This is why VC math produces lower valuations than founders expect. A top Australian seed fund expects 60–70% of portfolio companies to return less than 1x. The winners must return 20–50x to make the fund work. They are not being conservative — they are pricing risk.

**The levers you control:** The three variables founders can shift in VC Method negotiations are (1) the projected exit revenue multiple — cite current AU M&A precedents, typically 4–8x ARR for B2B SaaS, (2) the time to exit — faster is better, (3) the credibility of your path to the exit scenario — named acquirers, strategic rationale, and comparable transactions.

## Method 4: Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

**Best for:** Series A+ with 12+ months of revenue history
**Used by:** Growth equity funds, strategic acquirers, late-stage VCs

DCF analysis projects future free cash flows and discounts them back to present value using a risk-adjusted discount rate. For early-stage startups, this rate is typically 40–75%, reflecting high failure probability.

**Why DCF is rarely the primary method before Series A:** Revenue projections for pre-product or early-revenue startups are almost always inaccurate. Small changes in the discount rate produce dramatically different outputs. Australian VCs use DCF as a cross-check at Series A, not a primary signal.

**When it becomes critical:** If your startup has A$500K+ ARR and is entering a formal Series A process, expect to be asked to defend a five-year financial model with explicit DCF assumptions. Your model needs sourced assumptions, not "industry standard" placeholders.

The **BlockID Startup Valuation Index (SVI)** is a proprietary benchmark that synthesises inputs from multiple valuation methods into a single calibrated score. It maps directly to how Australian investors assess pre-revenue and early-revenue startups:
