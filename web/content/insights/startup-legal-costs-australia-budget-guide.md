## Startup Legal Costs in Australia: Budget Guide from Incorporation to Series A

Building a startup is expensive. Between salaries, office space, and product development, every dollar counts. But there's one area where cutting corners can cost you far more than you save: **legal setup**.

Most Australian founders underestimate legal costs. They incorporate thinking it's just a few hundred dollars, then later face disputes over equity, IP ownership, or regulatory compliance that cost tens of thousands to fix. The good news? With smart planning, you can legally de-risk your startup for under **$20,000–$40,000 AUD** from incorporation through Series A.

This guide breaks down exactly what you'll pay, what you can DIY, when to hire lawyers, and where to find affordable legal support in Australia.

---

## The Real Cost of "Just Winging It"

Before diving into the budget, let's talk about the hidden costs of skipping legal setup.

**Founder disputes** are among the most destructive events in early-stage startups. Without a proper shareholders agreement, co-founders can end up fighting over equity, roles, and decision-making rights. By the time lawyers get involved, the startup's momentum is lost, and the bill can exceed **$50,000 AUD** just in legal fees.

**Intellectual property problems** are equally costly. If your company doesn't formally own the code, design, or algorithm your team built, investors will flag it as a dealbreaker at due diligence. Fixing IP ownership retroactively means getting every past contractor to sign assignment agreements—which can be impossible if people have moved on.

**Regulatory penalties** add up fast. The ATO can impose penalties for non-compliance with salary sacrifice arrangements or privacy rules. ASIC can fine companies for inadequate director disclosures. A single breach can cost **$1,500–$10,000 AUD** in fines alone.

> **The legal horror story:** A Sydney SaaS founder skipped IP assignment agreements with contractors and a co-founder's legal dispute over equity. When due diligence rolled around at Series A, the VC walked away. Total cost: 18 months of wasted work, legal fees of $65,000 AUD, and a failed funding round. The legal setup would have cost $15,000 initially.

Proper legal setup costs time and money upfront, but it saves you multiples of that later. Now let's break down exactly where that money goes.

## Stage 1: Incorporation & Company Setup ($876–$1,676)

### ASIC Company Registration ($576)

The first step is registering your company with ASIC. This is non-negotiable and straightforward.

**What you pay:** $576 AUD (flat fee as of 2024 for standard company registration).

**What you get:** An Australian Company Number (ACN), the legal right to operate as a company, and the ability to open a business bank account.

**How to do it:** Use the ASIC website directly ([asic.gov.au](https://asic.gov.au)) or use a fast-track provider like **Startups Australia** or **LawPath**, which costs $700–$900 but saves you 1–2 days of admin time.

**Pro tip:** Don't rush to incorporate before you're serious. Many founders incorporate, then never use the company. Wait until you have co-founders committed and initial funding lined up.

### Registered Office & Compliance Address ($300–$800/year)

Companies must have a registered office in Australia. You have three options:

1. **Your home address** – Free, but not ideal for credibility (flags as micro-business).
2. **A virtual office** – $300–$600/year from providers like **Regus**, **Spaces**, or **WeSpace**. Includes a physical address, mail handling, and meeting room access.
3. **A real office** – If you're renting space anyway, use that address ($0 extra cost).

**For pre-seed startups, a virtual office is the sweet spot.** It looks more professional than a home address without the cost of actual office space.

## Stage 2: Co-founder Alignment ($3,000–$10,000)

### Shareholders Agreement

This is where many Australian startups fail. A **shareholders agreement** (or cap table agreement) defines:

- **Equity splits** – Who owns what percentage?
- **Vesting schedules** – When does equity actually become theirs? (Standard: 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff.)
- **Decision rights** – Who decides on major raises, hires, pivot?
- **Exit rights** – What happens if one founder wants to leave?
- **Anti-dilution clauses** – How are equity percentages adjusted in future rounds?
- **Drag-along and tag-along rights** – Protection for minority and majority shareholders.

**Cost:** $3,000–$10,000 AUD depending on complexity.

**Why it matters:** Without this agreement, co-founder disputes escalate fast. ASIC has no template for this—you need a lawyer who specializes in startup equity.

**DIY option:** Templates from **LawPath**, **Rocket Lawyer**, or **HotDocs** cost $500–$1,200 but require you to fill in the details correctly. Risk: misses edge cases that matter later.

**Recommended approach:** Pay $5,000–$7,000 AUD for a lawyer to draft a standard template, then use that same template for future hires. Amortize the cost.

## Stage 3: Employees & Equity ($5,200–$16,500)

### Employment Contracts ($200–$1,500)

Every employee needs a written employment contract. This defines:

- **Role and responsibilities**
- **Salary and benefits** (including superannuation — compulsory in Australia, 11.5% of wages)
- **Confidentiality and IP assignment clauses**
- **Termination conditions and notice periods**

**Cost:** 
- **DIY template:** $200–$500 from **Australian HR**, **Startups Australia**, or **Rocket Lawyer**.
- **Lawyer-drafted:** $1,000–$1,500 for the first contract (then reuse for future hires).

**Critical clause:** Every employment contract must include an **IP assignment** clause that assigns any work-product IP to the company, not the employee. This is non-negotiable.

**Pro tip:** Use a template for early hires (pre-seed stage), then have a lawyer refine it before you hire your 3rd–4th employee when risk increases.

### ESOP Setup & Cap Table ($5,000–$15,000)

An **Employee Share Option Plan (ESOP)** lets you give equity to employees and contractors without immediately diluting shares or triggering tax issues.

**What you're paying for:**
- Drafting the ESOP document (~$5,000–$8,000)
- Setting up the cap table structure (~$2,000–$3,000)
- Compliance with ATO rules (~$1,000–$2,000)
- Legal review by tax specialist (~$1,000–$2,000)

**Why it matters:** Without a proper ESOP, you can't give equity incentives. Worse, issuing equity ad-hoc creates tax complications for employees and the company.

**Cost-saving tip:** Many lawyers offer packaged deals: "ESOP + Shareholders Agreement + Employment Contract" for $8,000–$12,000 total (vs. $20,000+ separately). Look for this bundled pricing.

**Tools to manage it:** Use **Pulley**, **Carta**, or **Airtable** (free templates) to track your cap table once the legal structure is in place. These cost $0–$500/month and are infinitely cheaper than cap table disputes later.

## Stage 4: Privacy, Compliance & Policies ($2,500–$5,500)

### Privacy Policy & Terms of Service ($2,000–$5,000)

If your app or website collects user data, you **must** have:

1. **Privacy Policy** – Explains what data you collect, how you use it, and user rights (required by Australian Privacy Principles, ASIC rules, and **Privacy Act 1988**).
2. **Terms of Service** – Legal agreement governing how users interact with your product.

**Cost:**
- **DIY template:** $200–$600 from **Termly**, **iubenda**, or **Rocket Lawyer** (auto-generates based on your business model).
- **Lawyer-drafted:** $2,000–$5,000 for custom documents tailored to Australian law.
