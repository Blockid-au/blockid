## How to Calculate Your Startup Equity Split in Australia: 2026 Framework

The equity split decision is irreversible. Unlike a bad hire you can let go or a product feature you can roll back, the equity you give away in your startup's first weeks follows you all the way to exit. In Australia's competitive startup ecosystem, where investors scrutinise cap tables with increasing rigour before committing at seed and Series A, a poorly structured equity split is one of the top reasons otherwise fundable startups cannot close a round.

This guide goes beyond generic advice. It provides a working framework for calculating a fair, defensible equity split using Australian 2026 market context, real co-founder scenarios, and an explanation of how vesting schedules protect everyone.

## Why the "50/50 Split" Is Often Wrong

The appeal of splitting equity equally is intuitive: it feels fair, signals trust, and avoids a difficult negotiation. But equity is not about what feels comfortable in the founding meeting. It is about aligning incentives with the actual and anticipated contributions of each co-founder over the life of the company.

A 50/50 split is mathematically fair only when both founders contribute equal amounts of:
- Time (full-time commitment vs part-time)
- Capital (cash or assets brought into the company)
- Expertise (domain knowledge that directly creates value)
- Risk (what each founder gives up to start the company)

In practice, these are almost never equal. One founder typically has the idea, the domain expertise, or the customer relationships. The other brings the technical skill to build the product. These contributions are different in nature, different in timeline, and different in value to the business at each stage. A one-size-fits-all 50/50 split ignores all of that.

## The Five-Factor Australian Equity Framework

The most practical approach for Australian founders in 2026 is a weighted scoring system across five factors. Each factor is scored and weighted to produce a final equity ratio.

### Factor 1: Idea and Vision (10-15% of total weight)

Who originated the core idea? Who defined the problem and the initial solution hypothesis? This matters, but less than most founders think. Ideas without execution are worthless. The 2026 Australian investor community consistently values execution over ideation.

Weight this factor at 10-15% of your total equity calculation — meaningful, but not dominant.

### Factor 2: Full-Time Commitment (25-30% of total weight)

The most financially significant factor. A founder who works full-time from day one accepts a dramatically higher personal financial risk than one who keeps their corporate salary while the company gets started. This risk differential must be reflected in equity.

**Australian 2026 context:** With the average Australian tech salary at $130K–$180K, a founder who leaves full-time employment to work on a startup foregoes significant income. A co-founder who maintains their job while contributing part-time is accepting far less risk.

Recommended approach: If one founder goes full-time and one stays part-time, the full-time founder should hold at least 10-15% more equity.

### Factor 3: Capital Contribution (20-25% of total weight)

Has any founder contributed personal capital to the company (paid for software, cloud infrastructure, initial legal costs, or direct equity investment)? This "founder's loan" or equity contribution should be accounted for in the split.

**Australian tip:** Document any capital contributions immediately as either a shareholder loan (repayable) or equity contribution (in exchange for a higher share allocation). Informal "I paid for stuff early on" claims create legal disputes. Get it in writing via a [Co-Founder Agreement](/insights/startup-co-founder-agreement-australia).

### Factor 4: Domain Expertise and Network (20-25% of total weight)

Does any founder bring an irreplaceable network, regulatory expertise, or customer relationships that directly accelerate the business? For an Australian healthtech startup, a co-founder who is a registered medical practitioner with existing hospital relationships is bringing significant value beyond their time and capital.

This factor is highly context-dependent. For pure SaaS startups targeting global markets, it may be worth less. For highly regulated Australian sectors (fintech, medtech, agritech), it can be worth significantly more.

### Factor 5: Risk Profile and Opportunity Cost (15-20% of total weight)

What is each founder giving up? A 45-year-old mortgage-holder with three kids who leaves a VP-level role to co-found a startup is accepting fundamentally different personal risk than a 24-year-old who just finished university. Higher personal risk deserves higher equity recognition.

## The Australian Vesting Schedule Standard

No equity split discussion is complete without addressing vesting. A vesting schedule ensures that equity is earned over time rather than granted outright. This protects the company (and the other founders) if one co-founder leaves early.

**The Australian 2026 standard, as required by Antler, Startmate, and most seed VCs:**
- **Total vesting period:** 4 years
- **Cliff:** 12 months (no shares vest in the first 12 months)
- **Vesting frequency:** Monthly after the cliff (1/48th per month)
- **Acceleration:** 12 months single-trigger on acquisition (standard), double-trigger preferred by founders

**Why this matters:** If a co-founder leaves at month 10 — before the cliff — they walk away with zero equity. If they leave at month 24, they have vested 50% of their shares (12 months of monthly vesting after the cliff = 13/48, not 50% — clarify the exact schedule with your lawyer). The exact calculation depends on the vesting start date and whether cliff shares are included in the monthly run rate.

**ATO note:** Under Australian tax law, shares that vest under a standard co-founder arrangement may be subject to the Employee Share Scheme (ESS) rules if issued at a discount. Founder shares typically issued at a nominal value ($0.001 per share) to a founder who is also an employee create a potential taxable benefit. Obtain ATO advice before issuing shares.

## The ESOP Pool: Planning Ahead

Before your first external funding round, you must establish an **Employee Share Option Pool (ESOP)**. The ESOP dilutes all founders equally before the round, so it is critical to account for it in your initial equity split conversation.

**Australian 2026 standard ESOP pools:**
- Pre-seed / angel round: 5-10%
- Seed round: 10-15%
- Series A: 15-20% (expanded pool typically required by lead investors)

**Practical implication for equity split:** If you split equity 50/50 and then establish a 10% ESOP, each founder ends up with 45% with 10% set aside. If you split 60/40 and establish the same pool, founders have 54/36/10. Plan for the ESOP in your initial equity calculation.

## Common Equity Split Mistakes in Australian Startups

1. **Splitting informally.** "We agreed in a café" is not legally binding. All equity arrangements must be documented in a Co-Founder Agreement and reflected in ASIC company records (share register). Informal promises create bitter disputes.

2. **Not revisiting the split when circumstances change.** A co-founder who started part-time and transitioned to full-time may deserve a reassessment. Many Australian founders negotiate an equity "top-up" for co-founders who take on significantly more responsibility than the initial split contemplated.

3. **Ignoring the vesting cliff.** A co-founder who leaves at month 11 with no vesting is a clean break. A co-founder who leaves at month 13 with 13 months of vested equity is an ongoing cap table problem. Plan the cliff carefully.

4. **Forgetting about advisors.** Australian startup advisors typically receive 0.25% – 1.0% equity on a 2-year vesting schedule with no cliff. This comes from the ESOP pool, not from founders directly. Establish your ESOP before bringing on advisors.
