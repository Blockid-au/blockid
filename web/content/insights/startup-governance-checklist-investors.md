## Why Governance Matters Before You Pitch

In the Australian startup ecosystem, the difference between a term sheet and a polite rejection often comes down to governance. While founders obsess over product-market fit, revenue growth, and customer acquisition, institutional investors are conducting a ruthless audit of your legal and operational backbone. They aren't just looking for a great idea; they are looking for a business that can survive the scrutiny of due diligence without collapsing under the weight of its own administrative oversights.

For pre-seed and Series A founders in Australia, governance is not merely a box-ticking exercise for compliance. It is a signal of maturity, risk management, and respect for shareholder rights. When investors examine your company structure, director duties, and cap table, they are asking one fundamental question: "If we put $1 million into this company today, is it legally secure?"

A messy governance structure can delay funding by months, reduce your valuation, or kill a deal entirely. Investors know that a startup with poorly drafted employment contracts, unassigned IP, or inaccurate financial reporting carries a "toxic liability" that becomes theirs the moment they sign the cheque.

> **The hard truth:** 40% of early-stage deals in Australia face significant delays due to governance clean-up, with some deals collapsing entirely because of unresolved director conflicts or IP ownership disputes.

This guide cuts through the noise to provide a practical, actionable **startup governance checklist** tailored for the Australian market. We will cover the 15 critical areas investors scrutinize, from ASIC registrations to board minutes, ensuring you are investment-ready.

## The Foundation: Corporate Structure and ASIC Compliance

The first stop for any investor's due diligence team is the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) database. This is the public record of your company's existence, and any discrepancy here is an immediate red flag. Your company structure must be clean, active, and aligned with your funding goals.

### Company Registration and Structure
Most Australian startups operate as proprietary limited companies (Pty Ltd). Investors will verify that your company is registered correctly and that the share structure allows for the issuance of new shares to investors without complex shareholder consent hurdles.

Investors specifically check:
*   **ACN/ABN Accuracy:** Ensure your Australian Company Number (ACN) and Australian Business Number (ABN) are active and match your legal documents.
*   **Company Type:** Confirm you are not an older structure (like a company limited by guarantee) that is unsuitable for venture capital.
*   **Sole Shareholder Risks:** While a sole director/shareholder is common for early founders, investors will want to see a plan to dilute this immediately to introduce external shareholders and board representation.

### Director Duties and Appointment
Under the *Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)*, directors have strict legal duties. Investors will review the minutes of your initial board meetings to ensure that the appointment of directors was formalised and documented. Failure to document director appointments can lead to "de facto director" disputes where individuals are held personally liable without having formally accepted the role.

> **Key Insight:** Investors look for a clear separation between the "Founders" and the "Directors." Just because you founded the company doesn't mean you are automatically a director without a formal resolution.

## The Human Element: Employment, IP, and Confidentiality

The most valuable asset in an early-stage startup is not its cash or equipment; it is the intellectual property (IP) created by its team. Investors conduct a forensic review of who owns the code, the brand, and the product. If you have built a million-dollar valuation but the IP is legally owned by a founder's uncle who wrote the first lines of code on a laptop, you have no business.

### IP Assignment Agreements
This is the number one deal-breaker in Australian due diligence. Every piece of software, design, or strategy developed by your team must be assigned to the company.

*   **Founders:** Founders must sign a Founder's IP Assignment Agreement transferring all pre-existing IP created for the company to the entity.
*   **Employees and Contractors:** Every employee, from the CEO to the intern, and every contractor, from developers to designers, must sign an IP assignment clause in their contract.
*   **Timing:** The assignment must be dated *before* the work was performed or immediately upon engagement. "Retroactive" assignments are often viewed with skepticism by investors.

### Employment Contracts and ESIC
Australian employment law is complex. Investors will review your employment contracts to ensure they comply with the *Fair Work Act 2009*. More importantly, they will look for the presence of an **Employee Share Incentive Scheme (ESIC)**.

Under Australian tax law, offering equity to employees must be structured carefully to avoid excessive tax burdens on your team. The *ATO's* "Early Stage Innovation Company" (ESIC) rules provide significant tax concessions, but only if the scheme is compliant.

| Feature | Standard Employment Contract | Investor-Ready Employment Contract |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **IP Ownership** | Often vague or missing | Explicit assignment to the Company |
| **Equity Option** | Ad-hoc or verbal promises | Referenced to a formal ESIC/Option Pool |
| **Confidentiality** | Basic NDA | Robust IP and Confidentiality clauses |
| **Termination** | Standard Fair Work clauses | Enhanced clauses protecting company assets |

If your team is working on company IP without a signed assignment, you are effectively building a liability. Investors will demand a "clean-up" of these agreements before transferring funds, a process that can take weeks and erode trust.

> **Critical Stat:** According to AVCAL data, IP ownership disputes are the single most common reason for due diligence delays in Australian seed-stage investments.

## Financial Integrity: Reporting, Audits, and Cap Tables

Investors need to know that the numbers you present in your pitch deck match the reality in your bank account and your books. Financial governance is not just about accuracy; it is about transparency and the ability to track the "burn rate" against cash reserves.

### Financial Reporting Standards
While startups are not always required to have a full statutory audit until they hit certain revenue thresholds (often $50 million in revenue, $25 million in assets, or 100 employees), investors expect **management accounts** to be prepared monthly or quarterly.

These accounts must:
*   Be prepared in accordance with Australian Accounting Standards (AASB).
*   Clearly show Cash Flow, P&L, and Balance Sheet.
*   Reconcile with bank statements and credit card transactions.
*   Identify outstanding liabilities, including unpaid director loans or shareholder advances.

### Cap Table Accuracy
The Capitalisation Table (Cap Table) is the map of who owns the company. An inaccurate cap table is a governance disaster. Investors will scrutinise this document for:
*   **Unissued Shares:** Are there options promised but not legally granted?
*   **Missing Signatures:** Did all previous shareholders sign their share allotment agreements?
*   **Valuation History:** Is there a clear trail of how the company was valued in previous rounds?
*   **ESIC Compliance:** Are the employee options within the ATO limits for early-stage schemes?

Inaccurate cap tables lead to "pre-money" valuation disputes and can legally prevent the issuance of new shares to investors. If your cap table shows 100% owned by founders but you promised 10% to an advisor without legal paperwork, that 10% is an invisible cliff that must be fixed.

> **Pro Tip:** Use a dedicated cap table management tool rather than an Excel spreadsheet. Excel errors are the leading cause of cap table disputes in Series A rounds.

## Board Governance: Minutes, Decisions, and Strategy

The board of directors is the strategic engine of your company. Investors want to see that your board is active, informed, and capable of making high-stakes decisions. A "sleeping board" suggests a lack of strategic direction and poor governance culture.

### Board Meeting Minutes
Minutes are the legal record of your board's decisions. They are not transcripts of the conversation; they are the official record of **resolutions passed**. Investors will ask for the last 12 months of board minutes to verify:
*   **Approval of Financial Statements:** Did the board formally approve the annual and quarterly accounts?
*   **Share Issuances:** Was every round of fundraising and every option grant formally authorised by a board resolution?
*   **Delegation of Authority:** Who is authorised to sign contracts? Who can spend money?

Missing minutes are often interpreted as "decisions made in the dark." If you cannot prove the board approved a major contract or a hire, that contract could be voided, or the hire could be disputed.

### Director Conflicts of Interest
A robust governance framework requires a clear process for declaring and managing conflicts of interest. This includes:
*   **Recusal:** Directors with a personal interest in a transaction (e.g., hiring a cousin's company) must declare it and recuse themselves from the vote.
*   **Register of Interests:** A formal log of all declared conflicts must be maintained.
*   **Related Party Transactions:** Transactions between the company and directors (or their associates) must be arm's-length and documented.

## Data Privacy, Insurance, and Risk Management

In the modern digital economy, data breaches and operational failures are not "if" but "when." Investors want to know that you have a safety net.

### Privacy Policy and Data Protection
Australia's *Privacy Act 1988* and the Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme impose strict obligations on companies holding personal information. Investors will audit your privacy policy to ensure it is:
*   **Up-to-date:** Reflecting current business practices.
*   **Compliant:** Covering data collection, storage, and usage.
*   **Transparent:** Clearly explaining user rights and breach notification procedures.

If your startup handles sensitive health data, financial data, or biometric information, you may be subject to even stricter regulations. A lack of a compliant privacy policy is a major red flag for institutional capital.

### Insurance Coverage
A startup without insurance is a ticking time bomb. Investors expect to see evidence of:
*   **Public Liability Insurance:** For physical risks to third parties.
*   **Professional Indemnity (PI):** Crucial for service-based startups or SaaS platforms.
*   **Directors and Officers (D&O) Liability:** Protects the board from personal liability. This is often a mandatory requirement for Series A investors to protect themselves and their board nominees.
*   **Cyber Liability:** Essential for any tech company holding customer data.

> **Expert Insight:** 60% of Australian startups fail due to legal or regulatory liabilities within their first three years. Proper insurance and governance are your only shield against these existential threats.

## The 15-Point Investor Governance Checklist

Before you send your deck to an investor, run your company through this comprehensive checklist. If you can tick every box, you are in the top 10% of governance-ready startups.

1.  [ ] **ASIC Registration:** Company status is "Active," ACN/ABN matches all documents.
2.  [ ] **Director Appointments:** All directors have signed resignation/acceptance forms and are listed on ASIC.
3.  [ ] **Constitution:** A valid company constitution is in place and compliant with the Corporations Act.
4.  [ ] **Board Minutes:** Minutes from the last 12 months are formalised, signed, and stored.
5.  [ ] **Shareholder Register:** Accurate and up-to-date, matching the cap table.
6.  [ ] **IP Assignment:** All founders, employees, and contractors have signed IP assignment agreements.
7.  [ ] **Employment Contracts:** All staff have formal contracts with ESIC clauses and IP terms.
8.  [ ] **Privacy Policy:** Compliant with the Privacy Act and accessible on your website.
9.  [ ] **Financial Accounts:** Monthly/quarterly management accounts prepared and reconciled.
10. [ ] **Tax Compliance:** BAS, GST, and PAYG filings are up to date with the ATO.
11. [ ] **Insurance:** D&O, PI, and Public Liability policies are active.
12. [ ] **Cap Table:** Clean, accurate, and free of unissued options or informal promises.
13. [ ] **Debt Register:** All loans and director advances are documented with terms and interest.
14. [ ] **Conflict Register:** A log of declared conflicts of interest is maintained.
15. [ ] **ESIC Eligibility:** If applicable, the scheme is structured to meet ATO ESIC criteria.

## How Governance Impacts Your Valuation and SVI Score

It is a common misconception that governance only affects the *speed* of a deal. In reality, it directly impacts the *valuation* and the *terms* of the investment. A company with a clean governance structure can command a higher valuation because the risk premium is lower.

Investors use a **Startup Value Index (SVI)** to quantify these risks. Your SVI score is a composite metric that weighs your governance readiness against market benchmarks. A low governance score can lead to:
*   **Discounted Valuations:** Investors may apply a 10-20% discount to account for "governance risk."
*   **Stricter Terms:** Higher liquidation preferences or board control requirements.
*   **Due Diligence Delays:** Extending the fundraising timeline, which increases burn rate.

By addressing the 15 points in this checklist, you directly improve your SVI score. This makes your startup more attractive, shortens the due diligence timeline, and increases your leverage in negotiations.

> **The Bottom Line:** Governance is not a cost; it is an asset. A high SVI score signals to investors that you are a low-risk, high-reward opportunity.
