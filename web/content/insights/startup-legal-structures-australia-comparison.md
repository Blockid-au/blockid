## Startup Legal Structures in Australia: Pty Ltd vs Trust vs Partnership

One of the most consequential decisions you will make before writing a single line of code or signing your first customer is choosing how to legally structure your startup. Get it right and the structure becomes invisible scaffolding that supports growth, fundraising, and eventual exit. Get it wrong and you face costly restructuring, tax complications, and barriers to investment that can kill a deal before it starts.

Australian founders have more structural options than most realise — and more traps. This guide compares the three most common startup structures: the proprietary limited company (Pty Ltd), the trust (particularly the discretionary trust), and the partnership, with a focus on what investors expect and what the ATO and ASIC require.

> **Key Insight:** The overwhelming majority of Australian startups that successfully raise venture capital do so as Pty Ltd companies. This is not coincidence — it is the structure that Australian corporate law and investor norms are built around. Any deviation requires strong justification.

## The Pty Ltd Company: The Startup Default

A proprietary limited company registered with ASIC under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) is the standard vehicle for Australian startups seeking external investment. It offers:

- **Separate legal personality:** The company is distinct from its founders, limiting personal liability to the company's assets
- **Share-based ownership:** Standard equity mechanics — share issuance, vesting, options, preference shares — all work cleanly within a Pty Ltd
- **ESIC eligibility:** Only companies can qualify as Early Stage Innovation Companies under the ATO's tax incentive framework, unlocking 20% tax offsets for qualifying investors
- **VC compatibility:** Australian and international venture capital funds are structured to invest in companies, not trusts or partnerships

The Corporations Act imposes ongoing obligations including annual ASIC review fees (currently $310 per year for small companies), maintenance of a share register, lodgement of changes to directors and officeholders within 28 days, and retention of financial records for seven years.

## The Discretionary Trust: When It Makes Sense (and When It Doesn't)

A discretionary trust is a popular structure for family businesses, property investors, and professional services firms in Australia due to its flexibility in distributing income to beneficiaries at different marginal tax rates. For a bootstrapped founder who is generating revenue and wants to optimise their personal tax position, a trust structure can offer genuine benefits.

However, the trust structure creates severe complications for venture-backed startups:

**Why trusts don't work for VC-backed startups:**
1. **No standard equity mechanism:** Trusts issue beneficial interests, not shares. Options, preference shares, ESOPs, and convertible instruments all require significant legal adaptation.
2. **ESIC ineligibility:** The ATO's ESIC framework applies specifically to companies — trusts are excluded.
3. **R&D Tax Incentive exclusion:** The 43.5% R&D tax offset under the Industry Research and Development Act is only available to incorporated companies.
4. **Investor structuring complexity:** Most VC funds are prohibited from investing in trusts by their own fund documentation.
5. **Restructuring cost:** Converting from a trust structure to a Pty Ltd before a funding round is expensive, time-consuming, and can trigger CGT events.

The right use case for a trust is during the bootstrapping or pre-idea phase, where founders are generating consulting revenue and want tax flexibility. Once you decide to build a venture-backed product, incorporate a Pty Ltd.

## The Partnership: Rarely the Right Answer for Startups

A general partnership is the simplest structure — two or more people agreeing to carry on business together and share profits. There is no separate legal entity, no ASIC registration, and no limited liability protection.

For startups, the unlimited personal liability of all partners is the defining issue. If your startup is sued, incurs debt, or has tax obligations it cannot meet, every partner is personally liable — including their personal assets, their home, and their superannuation.

A limited partnership structure is used in Australia as the standard vehicle for venture capital funds themselves, not for operating startups. The Australian Venture Capital and Private Equity Association (AVCAL) has published best practice guidance on limited partnership fund structures.

**The only scenario where a partnership makes sense for a startup:**
Pre-incorporation, where two founders are testing a concept with minimal investment and revenue before committing to a company structure. This window should be brief — incorporate as soon as the concept validates.

## The ESIC Opportunity: Only Available to Pty Ltd

One of the most powerful incentives for early-stage Australian founders is structuring to allow your investors to access ESIC (Early Stage Innovation Company) status. Under the ATO's rules, qualifying investors in an ESIC can receive:

- A **20% non-refundable tax offset** on their investment (capped at $200,000 per year)
- A **modified CGT treatment** on gains (10-year exemption if held for 12 months or more)

This is only available to companies. Building your startup as a Pty Ltd from day one is a prerequisite for your early investors to access this benefit — a meaningful incentive that can influence whether an angel writes a cheque.

## When to Restructure

If your startup is currently operating as a trust or partnership and you are approaching a fundraising round, the restructuring process involves:

1. Establishing a new Pty Ltd company
2. Transferring assets from the old structure to the new company (potential CGT event — get ATO advice)
3. Updating all contracts, licences, and agreements to reflect the new legal entity
4. Notifying ASIC, the ATO, and any relevant regulators

This process typically takes four to eight weeks and costs $5,000 to $15,000 in legal and accounting fees. It is vastly cheaper to incorporate correctly from the start.

## Conclusion: Choose Once, Choose Right

For Australian founders building a venture-backed startup, the answer is almost always a Pty Ltd company registered with ASIC. The structure unlocks ESIC, R&D incentives, standard equity mechanics, and the investment compatibility that every external capital source requires.

Trusts and partnerships have their place in the Australian business landscape — they are simply not the right place for a startup that intends to raise external capital, issue ESOPs, or pursue an exit.

Incorporate correctly from day one. The $538 ASIC fee and a day of setup work is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.
