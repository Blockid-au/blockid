## How to Value an Australian Startup in 2026: The Scorecard Method

Valuing a startup before it generates revenue is as much art as science. For Australian founders preparing for their first fundraise, knowing how investors actually arrive at a pre-money valuation is critical. In 2026, one method dominates the early-stage Australian market: **the Scorecard Method**. Used by sophisticated angel investors, accelerators like Antler and Startmate, and early-stage VCs, the Scorecard Method is the most transparent and founder-friendly pre-revenue valuation framework in the Australian ecosystem.

This guide breaks down exactly how the Scorecard Method works, what benchmarks apply to Australian startups in 2026, and how you can use it to arrive at a defensible valuation before your next investor conversation.

## What Is the Scorecard Method?

The Scorecard Method was popularised by Bill Payne, a US-based angel investor, but it has been widely adopted by the Australian angel and early-stage VC community. The methodology works in two steps:

1. **Establish a regional baseline** — the average pre-money valuation for startups at the same stage, in the same geography.
2. **Apply weighted multipliers** — compare your startup across 5-7 factors to adjust the baseline up or down.

The result is a valuation grounded in market data rather than wishful thinking. For investors, it reduces subjectivity. For founders, it gives a clear roadmap of what actually moves the needle.

## The 2026 Australian Baseline Valuations

The Scorecard Method starts with a regional median. Australian market data for 2026 shows:

| Stage | Median Pre-Money Valuation (AUD) | Typical Raise |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pre-Seed (Idea + Team)** | $2M – $4M | $100K – $500K |
| **Pre-Seed (MVP built)** | $3.5M – $6M | $300K – $1M |
| **Seed (Early traction)** | $6M – $12M | $1M – $3M |
| **Seed+ (Proven PMF)** | $10M – $18M | $2M – $5M |

These figures draw on data from AVCAL's 2025-26 annual report, Startmate cohort disclosures, and Antler Australia's publicly stated investment parameters. The $3.5M median for a pre-seed MVP-stage startup in Australia is the anchor for a Scorecard analysis.

## The 5 Scoring Factors

Once you have your baseline, you score your startup on five weighted factors. Each factor gets a comparison multiplier: if your startup is average for that factor, you score 1.0x. Better than average scores above 1.0x; weaker scores below.

### Factor 1: Founding Team (30% weight)

This is the single largest factor in any early-stage valuation — and rightly so. In the Australian market, where deals close largely on founder reputation and network, a world-class team can double a valuation on its own.

Investors assess:
- **Domain expertise** — have the founders solved this problem before?
- **Execution track record** — prior startup exits, senior corporate roles, or deep technical credentials
- **Team completeness** — does the team cover product, tech, and commercial?
- **Full-time commitment** — part-time founders are penalised heavily in 2026

| Team Quality | Multiplier |
| :--- | :--- |
| First-time founders, part-time | 0.6x – 0.8x |
| Solid team, one gap (e.g. no CTO) | 0.9x – 1.0x |
| Complete team, relevant experience | 1.0x – 1.2x |
| Serial founders, prior exit or deep domain | 1.3x – 1.5x |

### Factor 2: Opportunity Size / Market (25% weight)

Australian investors increasingly look for startups with a credible path to global markets. A purely domestic-focused play with a TAM of $500M AUD is viewed very differently from one targeting a $10B+ global opportunity.

Key questions: Is the market growing at 15%+ per year? Does the problem exist in the US, UK, and Southeast Asia? Can the product be localised with minimal rework?

| Market Quality | Multiplier |
| :--- | :--- |
| Small/niche local market | 0.7x – 0.9x |
| Large AU market, limited global | 0.9x – 1.0x |
| Large AU market, clear global path | 1.0x – 1.2x |
| Global category leader opportunity | 1.2x – 1.5x |

### Factor 3: Product / Technology (15% weight)

At pre-seed, investors expect an MVP. At seed, they want evidence that the product is difficult to replicate. Patents, proprietary data assets, AI models trained on unique datasets, and deep integrations are all positive signals.

For Australian B2B SaaS startups in 2026, the benchmarks are:
- Idea only, no code: 0.5x – 0.7x
- Working prototype or MVP: 0.9x – 1.1x
- Launched product with users: 1.1x – 1.3x
- Proprietary technology with defensible moat: 1.3x – 1.6x

### Factor 4: Competitive Environment (10% weight)

A clear, well-differentiated position in a market with identifiable but beatable competitors is ideal. Investors worry most about markets where a US giant (Salesforce, Atlassian, HubSpot) could replicate the product with six engineers in three months.

Document your **right to win**: local market knowledge, regulatory expertise, embedded customer relationships, or a data advantage that compounds over time.

### Factor 5: Marketing, Sales & Partnerships (20% weight)

Traction is king. In 2026, even pre-seed investors want to see some form of validated demand: paid pilots, letters of intent (LOIs), waitlist sign-ups, or a meaningful number of design partners.

| Traction Signal | Multiplier |
| :--- | :--- |
| No customers, no LOIs | 0.7x – 0.9x |
| 2-3 design partners, unpaid | 0.9x – 1.0x |
| 1-2 paying customers, $10K+ MRR | 1.0x – 1.2x |
| $50K+ MRR or $500K+ ARR | 1.3x – 1.6x |

## Calculating Your Scorecard Valuation: A Worked Example

Let us apply the Scorecard Method to a hypothetical Australian B2B SaaS startup in 2026:

- **Sector**: Legal tech for Australian SMEs
- **Stage**: Pre-seed, MVP launched, 3 paying customers
- **Team**: 2 founders (lawyer + engineer), both full-time

**Step 1: Baseline** = $3.5M (pre-seed median, Australia 2026)

**Step 2: Score each factor**

| Factor | Weight | Your Score | Weighted Multiplier |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Team | 30% | 1.1x (strong domain, full-time) | 0.33 |
| Market | 25% | 1.0x (large AU + SE Asia path) | 0.25 |
| Product | 15% | 1.1x (launched, paying users) | 0.165 |
| Competition | 10% | 1.0x (clear differentiation) | 0.10 |
| Traction | 20% | 1.15x (3 paying customers) | 0.23 |

**Total Multiplier** = 0.33 + 0.25 + 0.165 + 0.10 + 0.23 = **1.075**

**Scorecard Valuation** = $3.5M × 1.075 = **$3.76M AUD**

This startup should target a pre-money valuation in the range of **$3.5M – $4.5M**, raising $400K – $600K for 10-15% dilution. That is a credible, defensible number to bring to an angel investor or accelerator meeting.

## Common Mistakes Founders Make with the Scorecard Method

**1. Using global benchmarks instead of Australian data.** A $5M pre-seed valuation might be below average for San Francisco but above market for Sydney. Always anchor to Australian comparables.

**2. Over-weighting the team factor.** Yes, team is 30% of the score, but a 1.5x multiplier requires serial founders with prior exits. First-time founders with no customers should not claim a team premium.

**3. Ignoring the competitive factor.** Founders often score their competitive landscape at 1.0x because they believe they have no direct competitors. Investors see this as a red flag — it signals poor market research.

**4. Presenting a point estimate.** A credible valuation comes with a range. "We believe our valuation is between $4M and $5.5M based on the Scorecard Method, compared to the $3.5M Australian median" is far more convincing than "$5M because that's what we need."
