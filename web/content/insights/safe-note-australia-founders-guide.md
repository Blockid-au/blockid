## SAFE Notes in Australia: What Founders Need to Know Before Signing

Raising your first external capital is a defining moment. For many Australian founders, the SAFE note (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) has become the default instrument for pre-seed and seed rounds, championed by Y Combinator and embraced globally. But the legal, tax, and regulatory environment in Australia differs from the US in ways that matter enormously before you put pen to paper.

This guide cuts through the jargon to give you a practical understanding of how SAFE notes work under Australian law, where they diverge from convertible notes, and what terms you must scrutinise before signing.

> **Key Insight:** SAFE notes are not natively defined under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth). They are contractual instruments that create future equity rights, which means the legal and ASIC compliance obligations sit with your company's constitution and shareholder agreements — not a standardised framework.

## What Is a SAFE Note?

A SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) is a contractual promise to issue shares to an investor at a future date, typically when the company raises a priced equity round, undergoes a change of control, or dissolves. The investor hands over cash today in exchange for the right to receive shares later, usually at a discounted price or with a valuation cap advantage.

Originally designed by Y Combinator for the US market in 2013, the SAFE was built for speed and simplicity. No interest. No maturity date. No debt on the balance sheet in the traditional sense.

In Australia, however, SAFE notes occupy a legally ambiguous space. Unlike convertible notes, they are not regulated as debt instruments. But ASIC and the ATO have specific views on how future equity rights are treated, particularly around the Employee Share Scheme (ESS) framework if SAFEs are issued to employees, and the CGT (Capital Gains Tax) event timing when conversion occurs.

## SAFE Note vs Convertible Note: The Australian Difference

### Good Terms vs Bad Terms: Quick Comparison

| Feature | SAFE Note | Convertible Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Interest** | None | Typically 6–10% p.a. in AU |
| **Maturity Date** | None | Usually 12–24 months |
| **Balance Sheet Treatment** | Equity-like (no debt) | Recorded as debt |
| **ASIC Filing Required** | Generally no | Yes, if secured |
| **ATO CGT Event** | On conversion | On conversion or repayment |
| **Investor Protection on Failure** | Low (no debt claim) | Higher (creditor status) |
| **Speed to Execute** | Very fast (days) | Slower (weeks) |
| **Negotiation Complexity** | Low | Higher |

The core trade-off is clear: SAFEs are simpler and faster for founders, but investors carry more risk. In the Australian market, where many early-stage investors are sophisticated angels or family offices, SAFEs are gaining ground but convertible notes remain common due to the clearer legal footing they provide under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth).

## The Key Terms in a SAFE Note: What to Negotiate

### 1. Valuation Cap

The valuation cap is the maximum valuation at which the SAFE will convert into equity. If your cap is $4 million and you raise your Series Seed at a $10 million pre-money valuation, the SAFE investor converts at the $4 million price — effectively giving them far more shares per dollar than new investors.

A cap that is too low punishes founders by giving early investors a disproportionately large slice. As a benchmark, Australian pre-seed SAFE caps typically range from $2 million to $8 million depending on traction and sector.

### 2. Discount Rate

The discount rate gives SAFE investors a percentage reduction off the price per share in the next round, regardless of the cap. Common discounts range from 15% to 25%. Be cautious of SAFEs with both a generous cap and a high discount — the investor will choose whichever mechanism gives them more shares.

### 3. MFN (Most Favoured Nation) Clause

An MFN clause in a SAFE means that if you issue future SAFEs on better terms, the earlier investor automatically gets those better terms. While this sounds fair, MFN clauses can create a legal obligation to upgrade multiple investors if you iterate on your SAFE terms — creating administrative complexity under the Corporations Act.

### 4. Pro-Rata Rights

Some SAFEs include the right for the investor to participate in future rounds to maintain their ownership percentage. This is worth negotiating carefully — overly broad pro-rata rights can constrain your future cap table flexibility.

## Australian Legal Considerations

### ASIC Reporting and Disclosure

Under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), if you are offering financial products to more than 20 investors in 12 months (raising more than $2 million), you may trigger a disclosure obligation requiring a prospectus or information memorandum. The "sophisticated investor" and "wholesale investor" carve-outs under s761G typically apply to angel and VC investors, but you must verify each investor's eligibility before issuing a SAFE.

### ATO and Tax Treatment

The ATO does not treat SAFE notes as straightforward debt. The timing of the CGT event depends on when the SAFE converts to shares. Upon conversion, the investor's cost base for CGT purposes is typically the amount they paid under the SAFE. If the conversion occurs after 12 months and the shares are held for 12+ months thereafter, the investor may be eligible for the 50% CGT discount — a material incentive for Australian angels.

For founders, the issuance of a SAFE does not trigger a tax event at grant. Tax obligations arise on conversion or on a future disposal of the resulting shares.

### ESIC Eligibility

A critical consideration for Australian startups is whether your SAFE-financed investors can access the Early Stage Innovation Company (ESIC) tax offset under the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997. ESIC status provides investors with a 20% non-refundable tax offset on their investment and a modified CGT treatment. However, ESIC eligibility is assessed at the time of share issuance — which occurs upon conversion of the SAFE, not at the time of signing. You must ensure your company meets the ESIC principles test at the conversion date.

> **Practical tip:** Engage an Australian startup lawyer before issuing any SAFE note. Law firms like Tisher Liner FC Law, Nexus Law Group, and Gilbert + Tobin have templates adapted for Australian law that differ from the YC standard document.

## Common Mistakes Australian Founders Make with SAFEs

- **Using the YC SAFE template without modification:** The US version does not address Australian disclosure obligations, ESIC timing, or Corporations Act requirements.
- **Issuing SAFEs to employees:** SAFEs to employees may fall under the ESS framework, triggering different tax treatment and ASIC obligations.
- **No cap — "uncapped" SAFEs:** While common in early US deals, uncapped SAFEs in Australia leave investor ownership undefined until a priced round, which can create valuation disputes.
- **Stacking too many SAFEs before a priced round:** Each SAFE adds to the "shadow cap table," and if you raise five SAFEs before your first equity round, the dilution at conversion can shock new investors and existing founders alike.

## SAFE vs Convertible Note: Which Should You Use?

For most Australian pre-seed founders raising under $1 million from sophisticated investors who understand the instrument, a well-drafted SAFE is faster and cleaner. If you are raising from investors who want downside protection through debt status, or if you are in a sector where lenders need security, a convertible note may be more appropriate.

The answer ultimately depends on who your investors are and what your legal counsel recommends for your specific structure.

## Conclusion: Sign Smart, Not Fast

The appeal of the SAFE note is its simplicity. But in Australia, simplicity on paper can mask real legal and tax complexity beneath. Before you sign — or before you issue — take the time to have an Australian startup lawyer review the document, ensure your investors meet the sophisticated investor thresholds under ASIC guidance, and model the cap table impact at various conversion scenarios.

The founders who get burned by SAFEs are not those who used the instrument — they are those who signed without understanding what they were committing to.
