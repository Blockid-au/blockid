## What Is an ESOP and Why Does It Matter for Australian Startups?

An Employee Share Option Plan (ESOP) gives employees the right — but not the obligation — to buy shares in your company at a fixed price (the exercise price) after a defined vesting period. Unlike shares, options have no immediate value. They only become valuable if the company's equity value increases above the exercise price.

For Australian startups, ESOPs serve three purposes:

1. **Talent attraction:** You cannot pay market salaries at pre-revenue stage. Options bridge the gap between what you can afford and what a talented senior hire can earn at a large company.
2. **Alignment:** Employees who hold options think like owners. They make decisions that build long-term value rather than optimise for short-term comfort.
3. **Investor signal:** A pre-constituted, legally compliant ESOP pool tells investors you understand governance. Missing or improperly structured ESOPs are consistently flagged in Australian seed and Series A due diligence.

## Australian ESS Tax Rules: Division 83A Explained

Australian ESOP taxation sits inside **Division 83A** of the *Income Tax Assessment Act 1997*, commonly called **ESS Part 7A**. The legislation was substantially updated in 2015 and again in 2022, making startup equity more accessible.

**The core tax deferral mechanic:** Under a qualifying ESS, employees do not pay income tax when they receive options. They pay tax only when one of three events occurs:
- The options vest and are exercised (they buy shares)
- They leave the company
- A 15-year maximum deferral period expires

**To qualify for ESS startup concessions in 2026, your company must:**
- Be an Australian resident company (or have a permanent establishment in Australia)
- Have aggregated annual turnover below A$50 million
- Have been incorporated for fewer than 10 years at the time of grant
- Not be listed on a recognised exchange
- Issue options specifically to employees — not to contractors

**The contractor trap** is the single most common ESOP mistake in early-stage Australian startups. Issuing options to a freelance CTO who has no employment agreement creates immediate income tax exposure for them and a structuring problem for you. If a key contributor is not on payroll, fix that before any option grant.

## How to Set Up an ESOP: Step-by-Step for Australian Founders

### Step 1: Determine Pool Size

The Australian market standard for seed-stage ESOPs is **10–15% of fully diluted shares, with 12% as the median**. This pool is established before investor money comes in, meaning it dilutes founders — not investors. Blackbird, AirTree, and Antler all expect a pre-constituted pool in this range.

| Stage | Typical Pool | Notes |
| :--- | :---: | :--- |
| Pre-Seed | 8–10% | Covers founding team, initial advisors |
| Seed | 10–15% (12% median) | Pre-dilution before investor entry |
| Series A | 15–20% (often refreshed) | Covers 18–24 months of senior hiring |

### Step 2: Engage a Startup-Specialist Lawyer

Do not use a general practice firm or a template from the internet. Your ESS deed must comply with **ASIC Class Order [CO 14/1000]** (or its successor instrument), which provides relief from the requirement to hold an Australian Financial Services Licence when issuing options to employees. A startup-specialist firm (Gilbert + Tobin, Piper Alderman, or a boutique like Doyles List-recommended startup practices) can produce a compliant deed for A$2,000–$4,000.

### Step 3: Board Resolution and Deed Execution

The board must pass a formal resolution adopting the ESS deed. This is not optional — it is the legal foundation of the entire scheme. The resolution must specify the total pool size, eligibility criteria, and maximum term.

### Step 4: Issue Individual Option Agreements

Each grant requires a signed option agreement between the company and the employee. This agreement specifies: grant size, exercise price, vesting start date, vesting schedule, lapse conditions, and any acceleration provisions. Verbal promises have no legal standing.

### Step 5: Maintain the Option Register

ASIC requires a separate register of option holders distinct from your shareholder register. This register must be kept current every time options are granted, exercised, or lapsed.

### Step 6: ATO Annual Reporting

Under ESS Part 7A, you must lodge an **ESS Annual Report** with the ATO by **14 August** each year, covering all grants made in the prior financial year. Missing this deadline incurs penalties and creates due diligence problems.

## Vesting Schedules: What Australian Investors Expect

The Australian startup market has converged on **4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff** as the standard:

- **Cliff:** Zero options vest until 12 months of continuous employment are complete
- **Post-cliff:** 1/48th of the total grant vests each month for the remaining 36 months
- **Acceleration:** Double-trigger is investor-preferred (change of control AND termination without cause both required)

Single-trigger acceleration — where options fully vest on any acquisition — is viewed negatively by most AU VCs because it can complicate M&A negotiations.

## Common ESOP Mistakes Australian Founders Make

**Mistake 1: Options to contractors, not employees.** ESS Part 7A concessions are employee-only. Issuing to contractors creates immediate tax exposure for the recipient.

**Mistake 2: Exercise price below market value.** The exercise price must equal at least the market value of shares at the time of grant for a qualifying scheme. Setting it artificially low — especially just before a funding round — can trigger immediate taxation.

**Mistake 3: No board minutes for grants.** Every grant requires a board resolution. Without it, the grant has no legal standing and cannot be relied upon in any due diligence process.

**Mistake 4: Missing good leaver / bad leaver provisions.** What happens to vested and unvested options when someone leaves? Without explicit provisions in the deed, this becomes a dispute at the worst possible time.

**Mistake 5: Setting up ESOP after the funding round closes.** Investors expect to see a pre-constituted ESOP pool before they invest. Building it retroactively is a red flag and creates structural complexity.

**Mistake 6: Skipping ATO annual reporting.** The ATO ESS Annual Report is mandatory. Missing it is a due diligence problem during Series A.
