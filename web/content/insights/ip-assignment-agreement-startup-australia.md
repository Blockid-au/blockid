## IP Assignment for Australian Startups: Protecting Your Intellectual Property

You have spent months building your product. Your code runs cleanly, your design is polished, and your first customers are using it. Then you sit down with an investor's lawyer during due diligence and they ask the question that stops deals cold: "Who actually owns the IP?"

For too many Australian founders, the honest answer is "probably us, but we haven't documented it." That answer is not good enough when millions of dollars and years of work are on the line.

Intellectual property assignment is the process by which the legal ownership of IP — code, designs, inventions, trade secrets, brand assets — is formally transferred to the company that will commercialise it. In Australia, without a formal written assignment, the default legal position under the Copyright Act 1968 (Cth) and related legislation means that the person who created the work owns it. Not the company.

This guide explains what IP assignment is, why it is critical, what must be covered, and how to structure it correctly under Australian law.

> **Key Insight:** IP assignment is the most common due diligence failure in Australian pre-seed deals. Investors see it in roughly 40% of first-time founder teams — and it is entirely preventable with a single document signed before any meaningful work begins.

## Why IP Assignment Is a Legal Requirement, Not a Formality

Under the **Copyright Act 1968 (Cth)**, copyright in an original work vests in the author at the moment of creation — automatically, with no registration required. For software code, this means the developer who writes the code owns it, even if they are being paid by your company. For designs, the designer owns them. For written content, the writer owns it.

The exception is work created by an employee in the course of their employment — in that case, the employer (the company) owns the copyright under s35(6) of the Copyright Act. But this exception applies only to direct employees, not to contractors, freelancers, co-founders who worked before incorporation, or advisors.

In practice, this means:

- **A co-founder who built the MVP before the company was incorporated personally owns that code.** The company has no legal right to licence, sell, or modify it without their permission.
- **A contractor who built a core feature under a verbal engagement owns their work.** Your development contract must explicitly assign ownership to the company.
- **An advisor who designed your brand identity owns the visual assets.** Without an assignment clause in their agreement, you cannot register a trademark based on that design.

## What Must Be Covered in an IP Assignment Agreement

### The Four Essential Elements

A complete IP assignment agreement for an Australian startup must cover:

**1. Scope of IP transferred**
The agreement must clearly define what IP is being assigned — including all past work (code written before incorporation), all current work, and all future work created in connection with the company's business. Vague language like "all IP related to the business" is inadequate; specific categories (software, designs, trademarks, trade secrets, know-how, patents) should be listed.

**2. Consideration**
Under Australian contract law, a valid assignment must include consideration — something of value exchanged for the IP. For founders, this is typically their founder shares. For contractors, it is their fee. For employees, it is embedded in their employment agreement. An assignment without consideration may not be enforceable.

**3. Moral rights waiver**
Under the Copyright Act, Australian creators have "moral rights" — including the right to be attributed as author and the right not to have their work treated in a derogatory manner. These rights cannot be transferred, but they can be waived. Your assignment agreement should include a moral rights waiver from all assignors.

**4. Warranties and representations**
The assignor should warrant that they are the rightful owner of the IP, that no third-party rights encumber it, and that the IP does not infringe any third-party intellectual property.

## The Open Source Trap: A Technical IP Risk

Many Australian startups build on open-source libraries and frameworks. Most open-source licences (MIT, Apache 2.0, BSD) are permissive and compatible with commercial use. However, "copyleft" licences — particularly the GNU General Public Licence (GPL) and its variants — require that any software incorporating GPL code must also be released under the GPL.

If your product incorporates GPL code and you have not disclosed it, this can:
- Void your IP ownership claim over the affected codebase
- Require you to open-source your proprietary product
- Kill an acquisition because the acquirer cannot legally take ownership of contaminated code

Conduct a licence audit of all third-party libraries in your codebase before you enter due diligence. Tools like FOSSA, Snyk, and WhiteSource can automate this.

## IP Assignment vs Employment Contract: The Difference

A standard employment contract in Australia should contain an IP assignment clause specifying that all work created by the employee in connection with their employment belongs to the company. This clause supplements the default rule under the Copyright Act (which covers "course of employment" work) by extending to adjacent work and inventions.

However, employment contracts are forward-looking. They do not cover work done before the employment relationship began. A developer who built features as a contractor before being hired as an employee needs a separate assignment agreement covering that pre-employment period.

## Patents and Trade Marks: Registration Matters

Unlike copyright, patents and trademarks require active registration to gain protection:

- **Patents** (IP Australia): Cover novel inventions, processes, or formulas. A standard patent takes 4–6 years to grant and costs $10,000–$50,000+. Provisional patent applications can be filed for approximately $2,000–$5,000 and provide 12 months of provisional protection while you assess commercial viability.
- **Trade Marks** (IP Australia): Protect your brand name, logo, and other identifiers. Registration costs approximately $250–$330 per class of goods/services and takes 7–12 months. Trade mark registration is strongly recommended before raising external capital — your brand is a material asset.

Both types of IP must be owned by the company, not individual founders, for them to be validly transferred in an acquisition.

## Conclusion: IP Assignment Is the Foundation, Not an Afterthought

Every day you operate without proper IP assignment agreements in place is a day you are building a company on an uncertain legal foundation. For investors, clean IP title is non-negotiable. For acquirers, it is the primary asset they are paying for.

The fix is straightforward: engage an Australian startup lawyer, get assignments signed by every founder, contractor, and employee, conduct an open-source licence audit, and register your trademarks with IP Australia. Done properly, this work takes two to four weeks and protects everything you build thereafter.
